    Question Bank & Practice¶

    The canon of system design questions is smaller than it looks — a few dozen prompts, most of which are the same five or six underlying shapes wearing different products. This page maps the canon to this site's case studies, names the shapes so you can recognize a "new" question as an old one in costume, and lays out a practice regimen that builds the actual skill: performing design out loud, under a clock.

    The canon, mapped¶

    The classics (asked everywhere, at every level):

    Question Case study The shape underneath
    URL shortener full walkthrough Key generation, read-heavy caching, redirects
    Rate limiter full walkthrough Algorithms + distributed state
    Notification system full walkthrough Queue fan-out, providers, idempotency
    Chat (WhatsApp/Slack) full walkthrough Stateful connections, per-key ordering, presence
    News feed (Twitter/Instagram) full walkthrough Push/pull fan-out, the celebrity problem
    Video streaming (YouTube/Netflix) full walkthrough Object storage + CDN + pipeline
    Typeahead / autocomplete full walkthrough Prefix structures, cached top-K
    Web crawler full walkthrough Frontier queue, politeness, dedup
    Ride-hailing / proximity (Uber) full walkthrough Geo-indexing, matching, location ingest
    Payment system full walkthrough Ledgers, sagas, reconciliation
    Key-value store (Dynamo-style) full walkthrough The NoSQL page as a design

    The DevOps specials (increasingly asked, and your home turf — the full argument):

    Question Case study Why it favors you
    Metrics/monitoring system full walkthrough You've operated the real one
    Log aggregation pipeline full walkthrough The volume math is your daily bread
    CI/CD platform full walkthrough Queue-DAG-artifact-trust, lived
    Distributed job scheduler full walkthrough Leases, fencing, exactly-once-ish
    Feature flag system full walkthrough Config planes and fail-static

    The shapes — recognize these and no question is truly new: read-heavy content (shortener, feeds, video, typeahead → cache/CDN/precompute); write-heavy ingest (metrics, logs, tracking → partition/batch/append); fan-out (feeds, notifications, chat → push/pull + amplification math); stateful connections (chat, gaming, collaboration → connection tier + pub/sub backbone); coordination-critical (schedulers, payments, inventory → idempotency + ownership + fencing); matching/geo (ride-hailing, delivery → spatial index + streaming location). When a prompt lands, your first silent question is which shapes is this made of? — most real products are two shapes stapled together (Instagram = read-heavy content + fan-out; Uber Eats = matching + fan-out).

    The practice regimen¶

    Knowledge accumulates by reading; the performance skill builds only one way — out loud, timed, repeatedly:

    1. Study phase (per case study): read the walkthrough actively — pause at each design decision, answer before reading on, run the four questions on each component yourself.
    2. Rehearsal phase: 48 hours later, take the same prompt cold — 40 minutes, whiteboard or tablet, speaking continuously to an empty room. The silence where your narration stalls is the exact spot to re-study. (Recording and reviewing one of these per week is the communication page's highest-ROI exercise.)
    3. Transfer phase: take an unmapped prompt (design Strava, Ticketmaster, a parking system, Google Docs) and run the full framework — the test is whether the shapes transfer, and ticket-buying (inventory + contention + the thundering herd on drop day) or collaborative editing (CRDTs/OT + presence) will tell you honestly.
    4. Mock phase: humans, ideally strangers — peers, mock platforms, or the strongest engineer who'll say yes. Ask them to interrupt, redirect, and push back mid-flow; handling the twist is a skill reading cannot build. Two mocks minimum before the real loop; the first one is always rough, which is precisely why it shouldn't be the real one.

    Cadence for a 3–4 week runway: week 1 — Foundations + framework + two classics studied-and-rehearsed; week 2 — four more case studies (mix classics and specials) + first mock; week 3 — transfer prompts + deep-dive weak sections (the rehearsal stalls tell you which) + second mock; final days — cheat sheets, re-rehearse your two strongest walkthroughs (confidence is a legitimate prep target), sleep.

    Staff+ altitude

    Adjust practice to the loop you're actually facing: Staff+ loops add design-review interviews (critique a flawed design — practice by running the four questions and the review lenses against sample architectures, out loud), deep retrospectives ("walk me through the hardest system you've operated" — structure your war stories as detection → decision → systemic fix, and have three ready: an outage, a migration, a design you reversed), and the whether-questions (build-vs-buy, sequencing, kill decisions) — practice by taking any canon prompt and answering only the Principal layer for ten minutes: what would you buy, defer, refuse? If that layer feels thin, the Staff+ callouts across this site are your syllabus — they were written to be exactly that.

    In the interview

    Question-selection meta, for when loops offer choice or interviewers ask "what would you like to design?": pick the prompt whose shape you've rehearsed, not the product you use most — familiarity with the app is worth far less than fluency with the pattern. And if you're offered a DevOps special, take it every time: the metrics system or CI/CD platform prompt hands you home-field advantage that no generic candidate can match — you'll be describing systems you've operated in anger, and that smell is the strongest signal you can emit. The site's closing advice for the night before: don't cram new material — re-read the DevOps lens and your two rehearsed walkthroughs, review the cheat sheets, and remember that the interviewer is hoping you're good. Go be what they're hoping for.
