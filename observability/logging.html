    Logging at Scale¶

    Logging is the oldest pillar and the one that goes wrong most expensively: at fleet scale, logs are a write-heavy big-data system that happens to contain your debugging context — the estimation page already did the math (10k hosts × 100 lines/s × 300 B ≈ 26 TB/day), and that math, not any tool choice, is why every logging decision is really a volume decision. This page is the discipline: structure, the pipeline, sampling, and retention as the actual architecture. (The full pipeline design is a case study; this page is the principles it assembles.)

    Structured events, or grep archaeology¶

    The foundational upgrade costs one decision: logs are structured events (JSON with a schema), not prose. {"event":"payment_failed", "trace_id":"…", "user_id":"…", "amount_cents":4200, "reason":"insufficient_funds"} is queryable ("payment failures over $40 by reason, last hour"), aggregable, and joinable; "Payment of $42.00 failed for user 123: insufficient funds" is a regex audition. The craft rules: one event per logical happening (not four lines narrating one request — the canonical log line pattern: one wide event per request with everything attached is the single highest-leverage logging pattern in existence, and Stripe-style shops build whole observability cultures on it); trace ID on every event (the correlation thread — non-negotiable); schema discipline (field names consistent across services — user_id everywhere, not uid/userId/user — or cross-service queries die; the data-contract argument, again); and levels with meaning (ERROR pages someone eventually, WARN is a real anomaly, INFO is the canonical line, DEBUG is off in prod and sampled on demand).

    Two safety rules with teeth: never log secrets/PII (logs replicate into object storage, dashboards, and vendor SaaS — a token logged once is a credential leaked to every downstream system; redaction belongs in the logging library, paved-road enforced) and logging must never block the request path (async appenders, bounded buffers, drop-on-overflow with a drop counter — losing 0.1% of INFO lines beats adding 40 ms to p99; the at-most-once choice, correctly applied).

    The pipeline: a log is a stream¶

    The universal shape — and it's the streaming architecture you already know:

    Emit (stdout/file, app never ships) → collect (node agent: DaemonSet — tails, enriches with metadata, batches) → buffer (Kafka-class: absorbs bursts, decouples producers from every consumer) → process (parse, redact, route, sample) → store (the tiered part) → query.

    The buffer is the load-bearing decision: error storms multiply log volume 10–100× exactly when you need logs most (the burst arrives with the incident); without a buffer, the spike hits the search cluster directly and your logging dies with your product. With one, retention-as-RTO-budget math applies: six hours of Kafka retention = six hours the search tier can be down without data loss.

    Storage is where philosophy splits: index-heavy (Elasticsearch-class — index everything, instant search, pay 30–100% index overhead and real clusters) vs. index-light (Loki-class — index only labels/metadata, brute-force scan compressed chunks in object storage at query time: 10× cheaper, slower needle-hunts) vs. lakehouse (logs as Parquet in the lake, SQL over them — the converging endgame for warm/cold tiers). The honest selector: how often do you actually search full text vs. filter by service+time+trace-ID? Most orgs over-buy indexing for query patterns that are 95% "this service, this window, this trace" — which labels cover.

    Sampling: keeping the signal, shedding the bill¶

    At volume, "keep everything" is a bill, not a policy. The mature posture samples by value:

    • Keep 100%: errors, warnings, canonical lines for failed/slow requests, anything with a sampled trace, security/audit events (those are compliance artifacts, not logs — separate stream, separate retention, immutable).
    • Sample hard: success-path INFO (1–10% — you need the shape, not every instance), DEBUG (0% until a flag flips it on for one service — feature-flag-controlled verbosity is the grown-up debug story).
    • Sample coherently: per-request (if a request is sampled, keep all its events across services — trace-coherent sampling), not per-line, or you keep fragments of everything and the whole of nothing.

    Retention is the design¶

    The estimation-page verdict stands: nobody greps a petabyte. Retention tiers are the architecture: hot (search cluster, 7–30 days — where incidents live), warm (object storage, queryable-slower, 90 days — where investigations live), cold (compressed archive, 1–7 years — where auditors live), enforced by lifecycle automation, with the deletion story designed in (privacy law makes "we keep everything forever" a liability, not a virtue — and user-keyed deletion from logs is exactly as hard as you fear, which is one more argument for not logging identifiers you don't need).

    DevOps lens

    The logging platform's own incident canon: the volume spike (a deploy ships debug-level logging; the pipeline chokes; other teams' logs delay — per-service quotas and rate-based shedding at the collector, plus a top-emitters dashboard so the noisy team gets the page, not the platform); the parse-failure silent gap (schema change breaks the processor; events drop unnoticed — dead-letter the unparseable and alert on drop counters, the DLQ discipline verbatim); the burst-with-the-incident (buffer sizing and shed-priority decided before — errors survive, INFO sheds first); and the cost archaeology (anomaly alerts on volume per service — the recursive-logging loop and the accidentally-TRACE'd service are month-end horror stories that velocity alerts convert to same-day fixes). And the ergonomic truth: during incidents, responders live in filter-by-trace-ID; make that path three keystrokes fast and the rest can be slow.

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) The canonical log line as org standard — one wide, schema'd event per request, shipped in the paved-road middleware; it collapses "which of these 14 log lines matters" into a single queryable record and halves both storage and confusion (the highest-ROI logging decision available to a platform team). (2) Schema governance across teams — shared field dictionary, CI linting on log schemas, data-contract discipline applied to telemetry, because logs are consumed by security, finance, and ML long after debugging. (3) The index-vs-scan economics owned as policy — hot-tier size, label taxonomy, and the query-pattern data to justify them, revisited yearly (the Loki-class + lakehouse drift is real money at scale). (4) Audit streams are not logs — separate pipeline, immutability (object-lock), retention set by lawyers, access logged itself; conflating them means either over-retaining everything at audit grade or under-protecting the stream your compliance depends on.

    In the interview

    The pipeline sentence, ready: "Structured JSON with trace IDs, canonical line per request; DaemonSet collectors → Kafka buffer — sized so the search tier can be down six hours — → processor with redaction and per-request sampling → hot search 30 days, warm object storage 90, cold archive 7 years; errors kept at 100%, success INFO sampled at 5%, and drops counted and alerted." That's the log-pipeline case study's skeleton in one breath. Probes: "how do you handle 100× log spikes during outages?" (buffer + shed-by-priority: errors survive, INFO sheds — decided in advance); "why not index everything?" (query-pattern honesty: 95% of queries are service+time+trace — labels cover them at a tenth the cost); "logs vs. metrics vs. traces for X?" (the workflow: aggregate questions → metrics, causal questions → traces, detail questions → logs). Depth nugget: the canonical log line — name it, describe it, and note that it merges the logging and tracing worldviews; it's the kind of pattern that makes interviewers write things down.
