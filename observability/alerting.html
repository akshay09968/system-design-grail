    Alerting Design¶

    An alert is a claim: a human must act, now. Every alert that doesn't meet that bar — the flapping disk warning, the CPU blip that self-heals, the duplicate from a second system — trains responders toward the one behavior that kills companies: ignoring the pager. Alert fatigue isn't an annoyance; it's a failure mode with a body count of missed real incidents, and designing against it is as much systems engineering as any page in this site.

    Symptom over cause: the first commandment¶

    Alert on what users experience (the symptom: error rate, latency, SLO burn), not on what you think causes it (CPU, disk, replica lag, pod restarts). The argument is mechanical:

    • Cause-based alerts are incomplete by construction — you cannot enumerate every cause in advance (the unanticipated failure is the defining one); the outage you didn't predict has no cause-alert waiting for it. Symptoms catch everything that matters by definition, including the novel.
    • Cause-based alerts fire without user impact — CPU at 90% on an autoscaling tier is Tuesday; paging on it is fatigue-farming. High CPU with rising p99 shows up in the symptom already.
    • The division of labor: symptoms page; causes annotate. Cause-level signals (USE metrics, replica lag, queue depth) belong on dashboards and in the diagnostic path — pre-gathered context for the responder the symptom woke, or ticket-grade warnings for genuinely predictive signals (disk-full-in-4-hours, cert-expiry-in-30-days — the small honest class of "act before users feel it" alerts, with hours-to-days of runway as the qualifier).

    The implementation gold standard is the multi-window burn-rate construction: urgency scaled to budget impact, fast-burn pages, slow-burn tickets, blip immunity. If your paging layer is "error rate > 1% for 5 minutes" thresholds, that page already showed you the upgrade.

    The taxonomy: three lanes, no blending¶

    • Page — human acts now, sleep interrupted: user-facing symptom, SLO-threatening burn, dead-man's-switch silence. The bar: would you be angry to be woken for this? If sometimes-yes, it's not a page.
    • Ticket — human acts this week: slow burns, capacity runway, expiring certs, growing DLQs. Auto-filed, owned, SLA'd — the lane that prevents next month's pages.
    • Telemetry — nobody is notified; it's on the dashboard when needed. Most signals live here, and moving alerts down into this lane is where fatigue reduction actually happens.

    The anti-fatigue engineering toolkit, applied to every page-lane alert: deduplication and grouping (one incident = one notification stream, not 47 per-pod pages — the correlated-failure shape means related alerts will fire together; group by cause hypothesis, route as one), inhibition ("datacenter unreachable" suppresses every downstream alert it explains), flap damping (hysteresis — the same fix as LB health checks, same reason), and routing by ownership (the team that can act gets the page — the service catalog is the routing table; an alert routed to someone who can't fix it is fatigue with extra steps).

    Every page arrives with a runbook¶

    The alert fires at 3:14 a.m. The responder's cognitive budget is near zero. The difference between 4 minutes and 40 of MTTR is whether the page carries context and a next action: what fired and why it matters (which SLO, how much budget burning), links — the dashboard, the trace exemplars, the deploy timeline ("what changed?" answered pre-emptively) — and the runbook: verification steps, the no-diagnosis-required mitigations first (rollback, flag off, failover, shed load), escalation path. The discipline that keeps runbooks alive: they're versioned next to the code, reviewed when the alert reviews, and an alert without one doesn't ship to the page lane. (The mitigate-first philosophy continues in incident management — the runbook is its first page.)

    And the meta-alert that guards the whole system: the dead-man's switch — an always-firing heartbeat whose silence pages, because the scariest failure of a monitoring system is quiet success-shaped nothing.

    DevOps lens

    Alert quality is a maintained property with its own feedback loop: review the pager weekly — every page classified (actionable? novel? grouped correctly? runbook helped?), with the two damning categories tracked as metrics: pages with no action taken (delete or demote the alert — each one is fatigue compounding) and incidents with no page (detection gap — the postmortem's first question). Healthy on-call arithmetic worth knowing: sustained page rates above ~2/shift degrade response quality measurably; a quarter of pages un-actionable means the pager is lying a quarter of the time, and responders recalibrate accordingly (that's how the real one gets snoozed). The lifecycle rule: alerts are code — reviewed at birth (page-lane bar, runbook attached, owner assigned), tested (fire it in staging; game days verify the whole page→human→runbook path), and deleted — the alert nobody remembers creating, firing at 2% precision since 2023, is technical debt with a pager attached.

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) The paging bar is a cultural artifact you own — "we page only on user-impacting symptoms and imminent-runway predictions; everything else is tickets and dashboards" written as org policy, defended against the entropy of every incident spawning three new cause-alerts ("add an alert" is the postmortem's laziest action item; the Staff review asks which existing symptom should have caught this first). (2) On-call health is an engineering metric — page rates, sleep-hour pages, and fatigue trends per rotation, reviewed like error budgets; a rotation burning people out is an architecture problem (too much blast radius per team) or an alert-quality problem, and both are fixable by design. (3) Detection time is a designed number — MTTR decomposes into detect + diagnose + mitigate; burn-rate windows, scrape intervals, and evaluation periods are your detection latency; a Staff engineer can state "we detect fast-burns in under 3 minutes" with the arithmetic behind it. (4) Alert routing mirrors ownership — if pages regularly bounce between teams, the alert taxonomy is telling you the service ownership map is wrong; listen to it.

    In the interview

    The design-completing sentence: "Alerting is symptom-based — SLO burn-rate, multi-window: 14× over an hour pages, 3× over six tickets; causes live on dashboards for diagnosis, not in the pager. Every page carries its runbook — mitigation first: rollback, flag, shed — and related alerts group and inhibit so one incident is one page. Plus a dead-man's switch on the pipeline itself." Probes: "CPU is at 95% — do you page?" (no — symptom check: is p99 or error rate moving? CPU annotates the diagnosis; paging on it trains pager-blindness); "how do you avoid alert fatigue?" (the lanes + the weekly review with the two damning metrics — systemic answers, not "tune thresholds"); "how fast do you detect an outage?" (burn-window arithmetic + dead-man's coverage — giving a number with reasoning is the operator's tell); "what's in a good alert?" (why-it-matters, what-changed, dashboard/trace links, runbook — the 3 a.m. cognitive-budget argument). This topic is where the DevOps lens pays most visibly: everyone has drawn architectures; few have been woken by them.
