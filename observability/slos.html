    SLOs & Error Budgets¶

    The service-level objective is the most consequential idea SRE gave the industry: it converts "reliability" from a virtue everyone salutes into arithmetic everyone can negotiate with. Done right, it ends the oldest war in software — features versus stability — by replacing opinions with a budget. Done as theater (a wiki page of aspirational nines nobody measures), it's just paperwork. This page is the real version, math included, because Staff+ interviews probe this vocabulary constantly and can smell the difference between fluency and flashcards.

    The three-letter ladder, precisely¶

    • SLI (indicator) — the measurement: a ratio of good events to total events. "Proportion of requests served successfully in under 300 ms, measured at the load balancer."
    • SLO (objective) — the target on that measurement: "99.9% over a rolling 30 days."
    • SLA (agreement) — the contract: an SLO with money attached (credits, penalties), owed to customers. Internal targets are SLOs; the SLA is always looser than the SLO (you page yourself before you owe refunds).

    The craft is almost entirely in the SLI. Rules that separate real ones from dashboard decoration: measure where the user is (at the LB or client, not inside the service — a service that's "up" behind a dead LB is down; the differential-observability lesson); good/total ratios, not averages (averages lie; "99% of requests under 300 ms" is a percentile statement with a denominator); per user-journey, not per microservice ("checkout completes" spans eleven services — users experience journeys, and availability composes multiplicatively across your internal hops whether you measure it or not); and few (3–5 journeys; twenty SLOs is zero SLOs with extra dashboards).

    The error budget: the number that changes behavior¶

    An SLO of 99.9% is, flipped over, permission to fail 0.1% of the time — the error budget: ~43 minutes of full downtime (or 43,000 bad requests per 43M) per 30 days. That flip is the whole invention, because a budget is spendable:

    • Budget healthy → ship faster, run riskier experiments, take the aggressive migration. The budget is fuel for velocity, not a safety margin to hoard — chronic under-spend means you're over-investing in reliability nobody asked for (each nine costs 10×, and the money came from somewhere).
    • Budget exhausted → the policy triggers: feature freezes behind reliability work, rollout waves slow, risk review tightens. Written in advance and agreed with product — that pre-agreement is the entire trick: the freeze isn't engineering vetoing product in a heated meeting; it's both sides honoring a treaty they signed in peacetime.

    Burn rate is the operational derivative: budget consumed ÷ budget accrued. Burn 1× = exactly on target; burn 14.4× = the 30-day budget gone in 50 hours. And it yields the best alerting math in the industry — multi-window burn-rate alerts: page on fast burn (14.4× over 1 h — a real fire, worth waking someone), ticket on slow burn (3× over 6 h — a smolder, worth tomorrow morning), ignore blips that self-correct. This one construction solves alert fatigue and slow-leak blindness simultaneously — a single bad deploy that 500s 0.05% of requests forever never trips a threshold alert, but it burns budget arithmetic visibly. If you internalize one formula from this section, make it this one.

    Choosing targets honestly¶

    The right SLO is as low as users tolerate, not as high as engineering can boast: derive from user experience (what staleness/latency/failure do users notice — the staleness-budget conversation, formalized), from business tiering (payments ≠ recommendations — uniform nines are uniform waste), and from measured current performance (an SLO wildly above reality is fiction; wildly below is a free pass — start near reality, tighten deliberately). Remember the dependency arithmetic: your SLO is capped by your critical dependencies' — a 99.99% target atop a chain of 99.9% services is a wish, and making that visible is half of an SLO program's value (soft-dependency design is how you escape the multiplication).

    DevOps lens

    Implementation truths: the SLI pipeline is production code — computed from LB logs or metrics with histogram buckets placed at the SLO threshold (or your compliance number is interpolation noise); versioned, tested, and owned, because a broken SLI silently corrupts every decision downstream. Dashboards show budget, not just rate — "62% of budget remaining, burn 0.8×, 19 days left in window" is a sentence an engineering director acts on; a latency graph is not. The budget attribution habit: tag burn by cause (deploy-correlated, dependency, capacity, unknown) and the quarterly review writes itself — "70% of burn was change-induced" is the progressive-delivery business case with your own data. And watch the window mechanics: rolling windows heal gradually (a bad day haunts you for 30 days — that's the point); calendar windows reset cliff-edge (and invite month-end risk gaming — rolling is usually the honest choice).

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) The SLO is a treaty, and you're its diplomat — the artifact that matters is the signed budget policy (exhaustion consequences, pre-agreed with product leadership); an SLO without an enforced policy is a dashboard with ambitions. (2) SLOs shape architecture money — "we need 99.95 on checkout, which caps us at N serial hard dependencies; everything else goes soft with fallbacks" is the sentence that turns an SLO into a design constraint; and conversely, "this tier only needs 99.5 — delete the multi-region line item" is the cost conversation SLOs exist to enable. (3) Guard against SLO theater — the smells: SLOs nobody can recite, budgets that never trigger policy, SLIs measured where convenient rather than where users are, and twenty objectives per service; the fix is ruthless pruning to the 3–5 journeys the business actually dies without. (4) Error budgets generalize — cost budgets, flake budgets in CI, latency budgets: the pattern (target → budget → burn alerting → pre-agreed policy) is one of the most reusable governance machines in engineering; recognizing it as a pattern is the Staff-level move.

    In the interview

    Fluency here is disproportionately valuable because the vocabulary is common and the understanding is rare. The set piece: "SLI: good-over-total, measured at the user's edge — say, checkout requests completing under 500 ms. SLO: 99.9% rolling 30 days, which is a 43-minute monthly error budget. Alerting is burn-rate, multi-window: page at 14.4× over an hour, ticket at 3× over six — no threshold noise, no slow-leak blindness. Budget exhausted triggers the pre-agreed policy: freeze risky rollouts, spend the sprint on reliability." Four sentences, complete system, arithmetic included. Probes: "why not just alert on error rate?" (thresholds miss slow burns and page on self-healing blips — burn rate scales urgency to budget impact); "who sets the SLO?" (product + engineering jointly — it's a user-tolerance number, not an engineering pride number); "what if a dependency can't support your SLO?" (the multiplication — make it soft with fallback, or renegotiate the target; naming the cap is the senior move); "99.9 or 99.99?" (the 10×-cost-per-nine answer, ending with "which journeys actually need it?").
