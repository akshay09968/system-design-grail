    Incident Management¶

    Incidents are not exceptional events to be survived; they're a workload — one every serious system carries forever — and like any workload, they can be designed for. The organizations that handle them well don't have calmer engineers; they have structure that removes decisions from the worst hour: pre-assigned roles, pre-agreed severity, pre-written mitigations, and a learning loop that converts each incident into system improvement. This page is that structure, plus the postmortem discipline that makes the pain pay dividends.

    The choreography: roles before heroics¶

    The failure mode of unstructured response is everyone debugging and nobody deciding — twelve engineers in a call, four theories, zero owner, while the status page stays silent. The fix is stolen from emergency services (literally — Incident Command System):

    • Incident Commander (IC) — owns the response, not the keyboard: coordinates, decides (mitigate now vs. investigate first? fail over or wait?), delegates, and explicitly does not debug. The IC's whole value is being the one person whose head is above the water.
    • Ops lead(s) — hands on keyboards, executing the technical response.
    • Communications lead — updates stakeholders and the status page on a cadence, freeing responders from the "any update?" ping storm.

    Small incidents collapse the roles into one person — but the roles still exist (you're consciously wearing three hats), and the structure scales up smoothly when severity does. Severity levels (SEV1: users down, all-hands; SEV3: degraded, business-hours) are the pre-agreement that sizes the response — declared early and generously: over-declaring costs minutes of assembly; under-declaring costs an hour of solo flailing before escalation. Declare first, downgrade happily.

    Mitigate first, understand later¶

    The reliability page's MTTR argument becomes doctrine here: during response, restoring service outranks understanding the failure. The no-diagnosis-required mitigations come first — roll back the deploy (it's the leading suspect by base rate), flip the flag, shed load, fail over, scale out — while evidence (logs, traces, a snapshot of the sick node) is preserved for the real investigation later. The discipline has a mantra worth quoting: "you can't root-cause a fire while it's burning, and you don't need to."

    Two structural aids make it real: the "what changed?" reflex (deploy markers, flag flips, config pushes on every dashboard — the majority of incidents are change-correlated, so the change timeline is diagnostic step one), and rehearsed decision trees in the runbooks — the failover that requires a debate has a 40-minute meeting inside your RTO; the one with a pre-agreed trigger threshold doesn't.

    Communication: the other half¶

    Incidents are trust events. The operational rule: communicate on cadence, not on progress — "investigating, next update in 30 minutes" beats silence even with nothing new, because stakeholders' alternative to your updates is their imagination. Internally: one channel per incident, decisions and timestamps logged as you go (this is the postmortem's raw material — the scribe function pays for itself the next morning). Externally: the status page (hosted off your own infrastructure, obviously), honest about impact, updated by the comms lead on the promised schedule. The companies famous for handling incidents well are famous for their writing during them, not for having fewer.

    The postmortem: where the incident earns its cost¶

    The incident is over when service restores; the value arrives only if the learning loop runs. The discipline that makes postmortems work:

    • Blameless, structurally — not as kindness but as epistemics: if people fear blame, you get defensive narratives instead of truth, and next quarter's incident inherits the cover-up. The axiom: the engineer who typed the command is never the root cause — the system that made that command catastrophic is (no confirmation on the destructive op, no canary on the config push, no guardrail on the query). Naming-and-shaming produces exactly one output: quieter incidents.
    • Beyond the five whys — "root cause" is usually plural and systemic (the cascade had a trigger, an amplifier, and a sustainer — three fixes, not one); good analysis maps contributing factors (why did detection take 20 minutes? why did the runbook not cover this? why was the blast radius fleet-wide?) rather than hunting one villainous line of code.
    • Action items that actually close — owned, prioritized, tracked like features, and reviewed for systemic bias: the lazy postmortem yields "add an alert, update the runbook" (the alerting page's least-favorite genre); the good one asks whether the class of failure can be designed away (fencing, canary analysis, bulkheads). Track the closure rate — a postmortem program whose action items rot is theater with documentation.
    • Shared widely — postmortems are the org's highest-density engineering literature; a searchable corpus of them is training data for every new on-call and the chaos program's hypothesis list.

    DevOps lens

    The operational details that separate smooth response from chaos: paging assembles the room (escalation policies with timeouts — if the primary doesn't ack in 5 minutes, it cascades; the IC role has its own rotation), tooling pre-staged (incident channel auto-created with the alert context, dashboard/trace/deploy-timeline links pre-populated — every minute of "what's the Grafana URL?" is MTTR), access pre-verified (break-glass credentials tested before the incident that needs them — discovering your prod access expired during a SEV1 is a genre), and the handoff protocol for long incidents (fresh IC every few hours with structured handoff, because judgment degrades faster than people admit). And the metric-honesty note: track MTTR distributions and detection-vs-mitigation breakdowns for learning, but resist making MTTR a target — Goodhart's law turns it into premature "resolved" declarations with reopened incidents trailing behind.

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) You're the IC bench — Staff engineers disproportionately carry incident command for SEV1s, and train the bench (shadowing rotations, game-day ICs — chaos exercises double as command rehearsal); an org whose only competent IC is one person has a single point of failure wearing a lanyard. (2) Postmortem review is architecture review — the quarterly read-across of incidents is where systemic patterns surface (four incidents traced to config pushes = the platform needs config canarying, not four runbook updates); mining the corpus for classes is the highest-leverage reliability work available. (3) The incident tax funds the roadmap — translating incident load into engineering priorities ("we spent 340 engineer-hours on cache-related incidents; the paved-road cache client costs 200") is how reliability work wins prioritization arguments with arithmetic instead of fear. (4) Practice is policy — game days, failover Fridays, and surprise drills are calendared, budgeted, and treated as non-optional; the org's incident response is exactly as good as its last rehearsal, never its documentation.

    In the interview

    This page surfaces two ways. As design vocabulary: any system you present should be operable under incident — "deploy markers on every dashboard, runbook-attached alerts, rollback as the default mitigation, and the failover has a pre-agreed trigger so the RTO doesn't contain a meeting." As behavioral ammunition (Staff loops love incident stories): structure your best war story as detection → assembly → mitigation-before-diagnosis → the systemic fix that removed the class — that arc, told in ninety seconds, demonstrates command, judgment, and learning-loop thinking simultaneously. Probes: "walk me through your incident process" (roles, severity, mitigate-first, cadence comms, blameless loop — structure, not vibes); "what makes a good postmortem?" (blameless-as-epistemics + contributing factors over single root cause + closure-tracked systemic actions); "your MTTR is 40 minutes — how do you cut it?" (decompose: detection alerting windows, assembly [escalation], decision [pre-agreed triggers], execution [rehearsed rollback] — attack the biggest slice, which is usually decision latency).
