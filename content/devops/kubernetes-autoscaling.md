# Kubernetes Autoscaling & Capacity

Everything about Kubernetes capacity hangs on two YAML fields most engineers set by copy-paste: `requests` and `limits`. Requests drive [scheduling](kubernetes-architecture.md) and cost; limits drive [throttling and OOM kills](linux-fundamentals.md); the gap between them is your overcommit policy, whether you meant to have one or not. Get these right and the three autoscalers compose into a system that breathes with your traffic; get them wrong and you get the modern classics — the cluster that's "full" at 30% utilization, and the p99 spikes no profiler can find.

## Requests vs. limits: the contract and the enforcement

**Requests** are the scheduler's currency: a pod requesting 2 CPU / 4 GiB occupies that much of a node's ledger *regardless of what it actually uses*. Consequences: cluster "fullness" is **sum of requests vs. allocatable** — not real utilization — so a fleet of over-requested pods produces the flagship pathology: *nodes 80% requested, 25% utilized, scheduler says no room, finance says why three hundred nodes.* The gap between requested and used is **stranded capacity**, and at scale it's often the single largest line item in the [cloud bill](cost-capacity.md).

**Limits** are [cgroup enforcement](containers.md): memory limit exceeded → **OOMKill** (exit 137, restart, [the walk you know](linux-fundamentals.md)); CPU limit exceeded → **throttling** — the process is *paused* for the rest of each 100 ms quota period. Throttling is the sneakier one: a multi-threaded service can burn its whole quota in a burst and then sit frozen — visible as p99 spikes with idle-looking average CPU (the giveaway metric: `container_cpu_cfs_throttled_periods`). Hence the widely-adopted posture worth being able to defend: **memory: requests = limits** (memory isn't compressible; overcommit = OOM roulette for the *node*); **CPU: set requests honestly, consider no limits** (CPU is compressible — contention degrades gracefully via shares rather than hard pauses; the counterargument is noisy-neighbor caps in untrusted multi-tenancy — [tenancy again](containers.md)). QoS classes (Guaranteed / Burstable / BestEffort) fall out of these settings and decide **eviction order under node pressure** — who dies first when memory runs short is a config artifact, decided in advance, by you or by default.

## The three autoscalers (different questions, different answers)

**HPA — more pods?** Scales replica count on metrics. CPU-based is the default and [often the wrong signal](../foundations/scalability.md) — I/O-bound services drown at 30% CPU; the strong versions scale on RPS-per-pod, queue depth/lag ([the leading indicator](../messaging/async-fundamentals.md)), or p99, via custom/external metrics (KEDA for event-driven: scale workers on [Kafka lag](../messaging/kafka.md), including to zero). The physics that HPA cannot repeal: reaction time = metric pipeline delay + stabilization windows + pod startup ([image pull](containers.md) + boot + warmup) — minutes end-to-end, so **spikes steeper than your startup time are survived by headroom, not autoscaling** ([the lag truth](../foundations/scalability.md), now with YAML). Scale-up fast / scale-down slow (stabilization windows) is the standard asymmetry — [scale-in is the dangerous direction](../foundations/scalability.md).

**VPA — bigger pods?** Right-sizes requests from observed usage. Its highest-value mode is *recommendation-only*: feeding the [stranded-capacity cleanup](cost-capacity.md) with data instead of vibes. Auto-mode restarts pods to resize (disruptive; in-place resize is arriving but young) — and VPA + HPA on the same metric fight each other; pick one owner per dimension.

**Cluster Autoscaler / Karpenter — more nodes?** Watches for *unschedulable* pods and provisions capacity. CA works in fixed node-group sizes; **Karpenter** bin-packs pending pods onto right-sized instances chosen from the whole catalog (including [spot](cost-capacity.md)) in seconds — effectively *continuous bin-packing as a service*, plus consolidation (repacking pods to retire underfilled nodes). The chain that matters end-to-end: traffic ↑ → HPA adds pods → pods pend → Karpenter adds nodes → pods start — **each arrow is latency**, and the sum is your real scale-out time; design headroom accordingly ([and provision buffer pods — low-priority pause pods that eviction converts into instant capacity — the warm-pool trick](../foundations/scalability.md), K8s edition).

## Bin-packing economics

The scheduler is solving a bin-packing problem with your money: node size vs. pod size interact (big nodes pack better but blast bigger on failure — [failure domains](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) vs. fragmentation; many small pods pack tighter than few huge ones); **fragmentation is real waste** (a fleet of nodes each with 1.9 GiB free can't schedule a single 2 GiB pod); and priority/preemption is the pressure valve (critical workloads evict lower-priority ones — which is also how buffer pods work). The [cost page](cost-capacity.md) picks up the money thread; the design thread to carry: *packing density, blast radius, and scale-out latency are one trade-space* — you tune all three with node shapes, headroom, and priorities, and "which did you optimize?" is a question with a right answer per workload class.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    The dashboards that run this layer: **requested vs. used, per namespace** (the stranded-capacity report — the single most actionable FinOps graph in Kubernetes), **throttled periods** (the invisible p99 killer — any Guaranteed-class latency-sensitive service with throttling > ~1% needs limits raised or removed), **OOMKill counts by workload** (limits vs. reality drifting), **pending-pod age** (the autoscaling chain's end-to-end health), and **scale-out latency histogram** (HPA decision → serving pod; if p95 exceeds your spike profile, buy headroom). Incident genres: the *full-but-idle cluster* (over-requesting — VPA recommendations + a request-honesty campaign), the *thrash loop* (HPA flapping on a noisy metric — stabilization windows, or the metric was wrong), the *cold-start spiral* (scale-up too slow → latency → more traffic queued → [metastable](../distributed/failure-modes.md) — headroom and [shedding](../distributed/resilience.md) break it), and the *spot reclaim wave* (Karpenter + spot needs [PDBs](kubernetes-workloads.md), graceful drain, and diversity across pools — [the spot page's](cost-capacity.md) discipline).

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Request honesty is a governance program, not a config** — teams over-request out of rational self-protection (throttling and OOMs punish under-requesting *them*, stranded capacity punishes *the company*); fixing the incentive needs showback/chargeback ([cost page](cost-capacity.md)), VPA-informed defaults, and platform-set guardrails — a Staff-level org design problem wearing YAML. (2) **Publish the limits doctrine** — memory req=limit, CPU-limit policy per tenancy tier, QoS/eviction expectations — as platform doctrine with rationale, ending the per-team folklore. (3) **Capacity headroom is a priced SLO input** — "we hold 30% node headroom because our spike profile beats our 4-minute scale-out" is a sentence connecting [reliability math](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) to the bill; owning it is the job. (4) **Autoscaling is not a substitute for capacity planning** — it's the *execution layer* of a plan that must still exist (quotas, instance availability, [lead times](cost-capacity.md), regional constraints); the autoscaler that hits an account quota at peak is a postmortem with your name in it.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    This page arms the "how does it scale, operationally?" layer of any design: *"HPA on queue depth — leading indicator, not CPU; scale-up fast, scale-down slow with stabilization; Karpenter right-sizes nodes underneath and consolidates; headroom covers the 3-minute end-to-end scale-out because the spike profile is steeper; memory requests equal limits, CPU uncapped for the latency tier."* Probes to expect: *"requests vs. limits?"* (scheduling currency vs. cgroup enforcement — then the throttling-p99 story, which most candidates have never heard of and every strong interviewer has lived); *"why is the cluster full at 25% utilization?"* (requested-vs-used gap — stranded capacity, request honesty, VPA); *"autoscaling didn't save us from the spike — why?"* (the latency chain: metrics → HPA → pull → boot → warm; headroom and shedding cover what reaction time can't); *"who dies first under memory pressure?"* (QoS classes — and the point that eviction order is a *designed* policy). The meta-signal: treating autoscaling as a *system with latencies and failure modes* rather than a checkbox — that's the operator's edge, verbatim.
