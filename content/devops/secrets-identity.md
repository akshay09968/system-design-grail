# Secrets & Identity

Read enough breach postmortems and one character keeps appearing: a credential — in a repo, in a CI variable, in an env var dumped to a log, minted years ago with god-rights and never rotated. Secrets management is the discipline of shrinking that character's role; **workload identity** is the plot twist where it's written out entirely. This page runs the arc: the lifecycle, the store, the dynamic upgrade, and the endgame where services don't *have* secrets — they *prove who they are* and receive short-lived capability.

## The lifecycle, and where it breaks

Every secret has five phases — creation, **distribution** (the hard one), rotation, revocation, audit — and the classic anti-patterns are all distribution shortcuts with deferred bills:

- **In git** — even private repos: history is forever, forks multiply copies, and an entire scanner industry (yours and the attackers') exists because this keeps happening. A leaked-then-deleted secret is leaked; the commit is the incident.
- **In CI/CD variables** — [the pipeline is the most-attacked, most-privileged system you run](cicd.md); its variable store is where org-wide cloud keys traditionally accumulate, which is why supply-chain attacks harvest it first.
- **In env vars** — the pragmatic default, honestly caveated: visible to `/proc`, child processes, crash dumps, and the debug endpoint someone left verbose. Acceptable *transport* for short-lived values; poor *residence* for long-lived ones.
- **Baked into images** — an [immutable, content-addressed](containers.md), widely-replicated copy of your password. The registry is now a secret store with none of the properties of one.

## The central store, and the two-key trick

A real secrets platform (Vault-class, or the cloud secret managers) buys four properties at once: **encryption at rest with access policy** (identity X may read secret Y — enforced, not hoped), **audit** (every read logged — your [incident forensics](../observability/incidents.md) for "what did the attacker reach?"), **versioning/rotation machinery**, and **dynamic secrets** (below). The distinction worth keeping crisp: **KMS** does *key operations* (encrypt/decrypt/sign with keys that never leave the HSM boundary); **Vault-class systems** do *secret lifecycle* (storage, policy, leasing, brokering) — and use a KMS underneath.

Underneath nearly everything sits **envelope encryption**, worth explaining well because it recurs everywhere ([S3](../data/object-storage.md), disk encryption, TLS session keys, etcd encryption): data is encrypted with a locally-generated **data key (DEK)**; the DEK is encrypted with the **master key (KEK)** in the KMS; you store ciphertext + wrapped-DEK together. Decryption asks the KMS to unwrap one small key rather than stream your gigabytes through it — centralized control, distributed performance, and *revocation by key destruction* (crypto-shredding: destroy the KEK and every dependent ciphertext becomes noise — [the deletion story](../data/object-storage.md) compliance actually accepts).

**Dynamic secrets** are the store's power move: instead of storing *the* database password, Vault holds a privileged credential and **mints per-service, per-TTL credentials on demand** — service A gets a unique DB user valid for 24 hours. The leak window collapses to the TTL, blast radius to one identity, and audit gains per-consumer attribution. The operational bill you should name alongside it: credential churn (DB user sprawl, [connection pools that must re-authenticate on rotation](../networking/fundamentals.md)) and the store's availability becoming everyone's dependency (see ops callout).

## Workload identity: the endgame

The deepest fix reframes the problem: *distribution is only hard because we're distributing durable secrets.* Stop. Let workloads **prove what they are** — attested by the platform running them — and exchange that proof for **short-lived credentials**:

- **Cloud-native**: instance/pod identity via metadata service → IAM role → auto-rotating tokens. Nobody ever *saw* a credential.
- **Kubernetes**: service-account tokens, OIDC-federated to cloud IAM (IRSA-style) — pod ⟶ "I am `payments/prod-sa`" ⟶ scoped cloud permissions, no stored keys.
- **[CI pipelines](cicd.md)**: OIDC federation — the job proves "I am repo X, branch main, workflow deploy" and receives minutes-lived deploy credentials. This one pattern deletes the industry's most-breached secret store (CI variables holding cloud keys).
- **Service-to-service**: SPIFFE-style identities in [mTLS certificates](service-mesh.md), auto-issued and rotated by the platform — identity as a property of the workload, not a file it carries.

The maturity ladder to carry into designs: *shared static secrets → per-service secrets in a store → dynamic short-TTL secrets → attested workload identity.* Each rung shrinks lifetime, blast radius, and human handling; the top rung's slogan earns its keep in interviews: **the best secret is the one that doesn't exist.**

## Rotation as engineering, not ceremony

Rotation fails in production for one structural reason: **single-credential systems can't rotate without a gap.** The fix is the [expand–contract pattern](../data/sql-at-scale.md) for credentials — **dual validity**: issue new, roll consumers over gradually, revoke old *after* usage of it hits zero. Which implies the prerequisite nobody budgets: **usage attribution** — you cannot safely revoke what you cannot prove is unused (the "rotation broke prod because team X's cron still used the old key" genre exists precisely where audit logs don't). And the philosophical upgrade that makes rotation *stay* healthy: **short lifetimes as a forcing function** — 90-day certs and hour-long tokens make rotation mandatory-and-automated instead of annual-and-terrifying; if rotation hurts, do it more often, with machines. (Revocation infrastructure — CRLs, OCSP — remains the weak sibling; short TTLs are the industry's honest answer: expiry beats revocation.)

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    The operational canon: **certificate expiry is the most preventable outage in the industry** — inventory every cert (edge, internal, mesh, webhooks, [the K8s control plane's own](kubernetes-architecture.md)), alert at 30 days, automate renewal (cert-manager-class), and treat a manual renewal as a bug in the automation. **The secret store is tier-0**: if Vault is down and tokens can't refresh, the fleet degrades on a TTL fuse — plan its [availability](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) (HA, DR, unseal procedures rehearsed) like the [lock service it resembles](../distributed/coordination.md), and design consumers to *cache-and-continue* through brief store outages ([fail-static](kubernetes-architecture.md), again). **Secret sprawl inventory** precedes all improvement: scan repos and CI, enumerate env vars, map who-uses-what — the migration to workload identity is a campaign run on that map. And watch **auth-failure dashboards** during any rotation: the spike-by-consumer graph is your early warning that someone un-inventoried still holds yesterday's key.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **One identity architecture, org-wide** — a single workload-identity scheme (platform-issued, OIDC-federated, SPIFFE-flavored) that every service inherits from the paved road beats five teams' five bespoke auth schemes; identity is the layer where fragmentation compounds worst, because every pair of fragments needs a bridge. (2) **Solve secret-zero explicitly** — every bootstrap chain ends at "how does the *first* trust get established?" (platform attestation, TPM/instance identity, human ceremony); Staff design names the root and its threat model rather than hiding it under a config var. (3) **Compliance is this page** — SOC 2 / ISO controls translate almost line-for-line into: central store, least-privilege policies, rotation evidence, audit logs; building the platform right makes audit a report, not a project. (4) **Buy-vs-run for the store** is a [tier-0 dependency decision](../distributed/consensus.md): managed secret stores remove unseal ceremonies and HA toil at the price of cloud coupling — decide per your DR story, in writing.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    The high-yield question is *"how do services authenticate to each other?"* — answer with the ladder, ascending: *"Not shared static keys. Baseline: per-service secrets in a central store with audit. Better: dynamic short-TTL credentials. The real answer: workload identity — pods attest via platform OIDC, get minutes-lived scoped tokens; service-to-service is mTLS with platform-issued SPIFFE certs; CI deploys via OIDC federation, so no cloud keys live in CI at all."* Four rungs, one breath, and it pre-empts the follow-ups. Depth nuggets on demand: **envelope encryption** in three sentences (DEK/KEK/wrap — plus crypto-shredding as the deletion story); **dual-validity rotation** ("two valid credentials during the roll — expand-contract for keys"); and the ops-credibility war story slot: cert expiry (*"I alert at 30 days and treat manual renewals as automation bugs"*). If the design touches uploads, payments, or webhooks, volunteer where the signing keys live and how they rotate — nobody else in the loop will.
