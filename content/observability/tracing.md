# Distributed Tracing

A request enters your system and touches eleven services, three caches, two queues, and a database. It took 2.3 seconds. **Where did the time go?** Metrics can't say — [aggregation destroyed the individual](fundamentals.md). Logs know, but the answer is scattered across eleven services' streams. Tracing is the pillar built for exactly this question: the request's *causal story*, reassembled — and in a [fan-out architecture](../foundations/latency-throughput.md) it's not a luxury, it's the only way "why was this slow?" has an answer at all.

## The model: spans, context, and the story they assemble

A **trace** is one request's tree of **spans** — each span a timed operation (service call, DB query, cache lookup) with attributes, linked to its parent. The assembly trick is **context propagation**: a trace ID + parent span ID travel *with* the request (W3C `traceparent` header for HTTP/gRPC; [message metadata](../messaging/async-fundamentals.md) across queues), each hop emitting its span tagged with that context; the backend stitches the tree.

The one paragraph of literacy that finds real bugs: reading a trace is reading a **waterfall** — sequential spans that could parallelize (the N+1 staircase: [45 sequential DB calls that should be one batch](../data/nosql.md) is *visible* as a staircase), gaps between spans (time nobody claims: queue waits, [pool waits](../networking/fundamentals.md), GC — the invisible latency that [no downstream dashboard shows](../distributed/resilience.md)), and the long pole in a parallel fan-out ([the tail-amplification culprit](../foundations/latency-throughput.md), named and timestamped). Ten minutes with a waterfall replaces ten hours of cross-team latency archaeology — that's the pillar's entire business case.

The propagation fine print that separates practitioners: **context must survive async boundaries** — through [queues](../messaging/async-fundamentals.md) (producer injects context into message headers; consumer *continues* the trace — or your trace ends at the first queue and the [event-driven half](../messaging/event-driven.md) of your system is dark), through thread pools and async runtimes (context is thread-local in many stacks; executor wrappers exist because context silently dropping at the first `async` is the classic instrumentation bug), and through *your own* [batch/saga machinery](../data/distributed-transactions.md) (span links relate one consumed batch to its hundred source traces). [A mesh](../devops/service-mesh.md) emits spans for *hops*, but only the app can propagate context *through itself* — sidecar tracing without app cooperation yields eleven one-span traces, not one eleven-span trace.

## Sampling: the economics that make it real

Tracing every request at volume is a [second product-sized data stream](../foundations/estimation.md). Sampling is therefore *the* design decision:

- **Head-based** — decide at the front door (keep 1%), propagate the decision so the whole trace is kept or dropped coherently. Cheap, simple, and statistically fine for *latency shapes* — but it keeps 1% of everything, meaning it *misses most rare events*: the one-in-ten-thousand error has a 1% chance of being traced. Your worst requests are almost certainly not in your 1%.
- **Tail-based** — buffer spans, decide *after* the trace completes: keep 100% of errors and slow traces, 0.1% of boring successes. Exactly the right data — priced in infrastructure (every span must reach the sampler and wait; a stateful buffering tier with [its own scaling story](../case-studies/metrics-system.md)) and complexity.
- **The pragmatic stack** most orgs converge on: head-sample a baseline (1–10%) for unbiased shapes + *always-sample* on error/slow signals where detectable + [exemplars](metrics.md) linking metric spikes to whatever traces exist + **trace-coherent log sampling** ([keep all logs for sampled traces](logging.md)) so the pillars stay joined.

Instrumentation itself is a solved-ish problem — **OpenTelemetry auto-instrumentation** covers the standard libraries (HTTP, gRPC, DB drivers, queue clients) with manual spans reserved for business-critical interior logic; the unsolved part is organizational: *every* service must propagate, because [one non-participating hop cuts every trace that crosses it](#the-model-spans-context-and-the-story-they-assemble) — which is why propagation lives in the [paved-road](../caching/failure-modes.md) HTTP/queue clients, not in per-team goodwill.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    Tracing's operational truths: **broken propagation is the silent default** — the dashboard metric is *trace completeness* (spans-per-trace distribution by route; a route whose traces suddenly halve their span count just lost a participating service — alert on it, because nobody notices missing spans until the incident needs them); **clock skew mangles waterfalls** ([NTP discipline](../distributed/time-ordering.md) — spans that "start before their parent" are a clock problem, not a physics discovery); **the collector tier is real infrastructure** ([buffer, batch, shed](logging.md) — under incident load, traces burst exactly like logs; decide shed-priority: errors' spans survive, successes' drop); and **traces are the incident tool with the fastest time-to-context**: the mature runbook motion is metric alert → exemplar → trace → [trace-ID-filtered logs](logging.md) — three clicks, one causal story, versus four dashboards and a guess. Teams that drill that motion during calm ([game days](chaos.md)) use it reflexively at 3 a.m.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Propagation as platform law** — trace context in every paved-road client (HTTP, gRPC, queue, cron), verified by CI conformance tests, because tracing's value is superlinear in coverage (80% coverage ≈ 30% of the value: the dark hops are always where the mystery lives). (2) **Sampling policy as a written economic document** — baseline rates, always-keep rules, retention, and the tail-sampling build-vs-buy, revisited with volume; "we trace 1% and keep errors always, costing X, answering Y% of incident questions" is the sentence the platform owes the org. (3) **Traces as dependency cartography** — the aggregate trace corpus *is* your real service map (who calls whom, how often, how slowly — [the architecture diagram that can't lie](../devops/service-mesh.md)); mining it for drift against the *intended* architecture is a genuinely Staff-grade review input. (4) **Span semantics governance** — naming and attribute conventions (OTel semantic conventions, enforced) so "database span" means one thing fleet-wide; without it, cross-service analysis is [the schema-drift problem](logging.md) with extra steps.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    Deploy tracing at the moment fan-out appears in your design: *"eleven services in this path — so trace context propagates end-to-end, including through the queue via message headers; head-sample 5% baseline, always keep errors and >1 s traces; exemplars link the p99 metric to real waterfalls, and logs are trace-coherent so one ID pulls the whole story."* Probes: *"how does a trace actually work?"* (context propagation + span tree + waterfall — then the async-boundary fine print, which is the depth check); *"1% sampling — what do you miss?"* (rare errors, almost certainly — hence tail-based or always-on-error; knowing head-sampling's blind spot is the practitioner tell); *"the trace ends at the queue — why?"* (context not injected into message metadata — and the fix's location: the shared client library, not forty teams' diligence). The elite nugget: **the gap in the waterfall** — "the time nobody claims is the queue wait or pool wait; traces are the only pillar that shows *absence*." Interviewers who've debugged production latency will recognize the scar.
