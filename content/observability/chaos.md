# Chaos Engineering

Every claim this site has taught you to make — "the replica takes over," "the breaker opens," "the region fails over," "the cache loss is survivable" — is a *hypothesis* until it has run in production circumstances. Chaos engineering is the discipline of testing those hypotheses **deliberately, at experiment prices, instead of accidentally, at incident prices**. It is not "breaking things for fun"; it's the scientific method aimed at resilience claims, and its maturity marker is boring: small blast radii, steady cadence, findings filed and fixed.

## The method (it's an experiment, not a stunt)

1. **Define steady state** — the measurable normal: [SLI values](slos.md), error rates, [queue ages](../messaging/async-fundamentals.md). No baseline, no experiment.
2. **Hypothesize** — *specifically*: "if one AZ's nodes vanish, checkout SLIs hold within budget because [topology spread](../devops/kubernetes-workloads.md) + [N+1 capacity](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) absorb it."
3. **Inject minimal, bounded failure** — smallest blast radius that tests the claim ([one pod before one AZ before one region](../foundations/reliability-availability.md)), with an **abort switch** and [error-budget](slos.md) guardrails (auto-halt on SLO impact — the budget is literally the spend limit for experiments).
4. **Observe, conclude, file** — hypothesis held (confidence earned, widen next time) or broke (congratulations: you found an incident *before* it found you — [postmortem-grade](incidents.md) finding, fixed and re-tested).

The hypothesis list writes itself from this site's claims: kill a pod mid-request ([graceful shutdown真?](../devops/kubernetes-workloads.md)); kill the [cache tier](../caching/failure-modes.md) (does the [dependency-cliff plan](../caching/failure-modes.md) hold?); partition the [lock service](../distributed/coordination.md) (do holders [fail static or fail stupid](../devops/kubernetes-architecture.md)?); expire the [leases](../distributed/coordination.md); slow — don't kill — a dependency ([slow is the harder case, always](../distributed/failure-modes.md): inject 400 ms latency and watch whether [timeouts, pools, and breakers](../distributed/resilience.md) actually compose); fail the [DNS](../networking/dns.md); revoke the [certs](../devops/secrets-identity.md); fill the disk ([the fourth question](../foundations/thinking-in-systems.md)).

## The ladder of ambition

- **Staging chaos** — free confidence, honest limits (staging never has production's data volume, traffic mix, or [correlated weirdness](../distributed/failure-modes.md); passing here is necessary, not sufficient).
- **Production, surgical** — one instance, [one canary cell](../foundations/reliability-availability.md), guardrailed. Where real confidence lives, because production is the only environment that *is* production.
- **Game days** — the sociotechnical tier: scheduled scenario ("region us-east is gone — go"), full [incident choreography](incidents.md) exercised: paging, roles, runbooks, [failover decision trees](../devops/multi-region.md), comms cadence. Game days test the *humans and runbooks* — which is where most real-world RTO actually lives — and double as [IC training](incidents.md). The findings are rarely "the database didn't fail over"; they're "the runbook's dashboard link was dead, the break-glass creds were expired, and nobody knew who could authorize failover" — [exactly the decision-latency components](incidents.md) no infrastructure test touches.
- **Continuous chaos** — the endgame: automated, randomized, always-on (the Chaos-Monkey lineage), which converts resilience from a property you *verify* into one you *can't regress* — teams design for instance death because instance death is guaranteed weekly. This is [the short-lived-certificate philosophy](../devops/secrets-identity.md) applied to failure: make the rare event common and the handling becomes muscle instead of ceremony.

**Prerequisites, honestly**: chaos without [observability](fundamentals.md) is vandalism (you must *see* the impact); chaos without [incident response](incidents.md) basics is scheduling an outage; chaos without leadership buy-in is a career experiment. Sequence accordingly — it's the *capstone* of the reliability program, not the entry point.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    The operational craft: **guardrails are the product** — every injection tool carries scope limits (tagged resources only), auto-abort on [SLO burn](slos.md), business-hours-only defaults, and an announcement protocol (surprise is for the *system*, not the on-call — unannounced drills come only after announced ones are boring); **findings are incidents-in-advance** — same [postmortem pipeline](incidents.md), same action-item tracking, same severity honesty (a chaos-found "we'd have lost the region" is a SEV1 finding wearing lab clothes); and **the cadence is the culture** — quarterly game days minimum, monthly targeted experiments per critical system, every [DR claim](../devops/multi-region.md) re-verified after major architecture changes (the failover that worked in January is a hypothesis again by June — [drift](../devops/iac-gitops.md) applies to resilience too). Start list for any org: kill one pod (verifies [lifecycle](../devops/kubernetes-workloads.md)), restart the [cache](../caching/failure-modes.md) in staging, inject dependency latency, run one tabletop game day. Four experiments, one quarter, and you'll have findings that reprioritize the roadmap.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Chaos validates the money** — every expensive resilience claim ([multi-region](../devops/multi-region.md), [N+1 capacity](../foundations/reliability-availability.md), [the cache-loss plan](../caching/failure-modes.md)) either has a recent passing experiment or is a budget line justified by fiction; "when did we last prove this?" is the cheapest hard question in architecture review. (2) **The program is portfolio-managed** — experiments prioritized by risk × uncertainty (test the claims you're *least* sure of on the systems that hurt *most*), findings fed into [the incident-tax arithmetic](incidents.md) that funds fixes. (3) **Blast-radius design enables chaos** — [cells, shuffle shards, canary slices](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) aren't just failure containment; they're *experiment containment* — architectures that can't host a safe experiment also can't contain a real failure, and the chaos program surfaces that isomorphism early. (4) **Sell it as insurance actuarial work** — "we spend N hours/quarter proving failover works; the alternative is discovering it doesn't during the outage" reframes chaos from cowboy theater to the most conservative engineering practice in the building.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    One sentence upgrades any resilience claim you make: *"—and that failover is game-day'd quarterly, because an untested failover is fiction."* It signals the entire operational worldview. The probes: *"how do you know your DR works?"* (the ladder: staging → surgical production → game days exercising the humans — with the honest point that decision latency and rotted runbooks, not infrastructure, are what drills usually catch); *"how would you introduce chaos engineering?"* (prereqs first — observability, incident basics — then announced staging experiments, then guardrailed production, cadence before ambition); *"isn't breaking production irresponsible?"* (the inversion: production *will* break — the only choice is whether the first test of your hypothesis is a guardrailed experiment with an abort switch or a 3 a.m. surprise; chaos is the *risk-averse* position, argued properly). And as a closing move in system-design interviews, the chaos sentence pairs beautifully with [the rollout move](../foundations/devops-lens.md): design, deploy gradually, *verify the failure story on purpose* — that's the full operational lifecycle in one arc, and almost nobody else in the loop will have said it.
