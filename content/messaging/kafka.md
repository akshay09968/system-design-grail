# Kafka Deep Dive

Kafka is chronically misunderstood because it's named like a queue and used like a queue, but it is not a queue. **Kafka is a distributed, replicated, append-only commit log** — the [WAL](../data/storage-engines.md) promoted from implementation detail to product. Hold that reframe and every design choice snaps into place: messages aren't deleted when consumed (logs are *read*, not *taken*); many independent readers replay the same history (analytics re-reads what payments already processed); ordering exists per partition (logs are sequential); and throughput is obscene (appending and linearly reading files is what disks and page caches were born to do). This is "the log is the database" — the idea running through [replication](../data/replication.md), [CDC](../data/analytics.md), and the [outbox](../data/distributed-transactions.md) — culminating as infrastructure.

## The model in five nouns

- **Topic** — a named stream (`orders`, `page-views`).
- **Partition** — the unit of *everything*: each topic splits into N partitions; each partition is a strictly-ordered log on disk. Ordering exists **only within a partition**; parallelism = partition count. Every Kafka design decision is downstream of this sentence.
- **Key** — records with the same key hash to the same partition: *per-key ordering* ([the scalable ordering contract](async-fundamentals.md)) — all of user 42's events, in order, forever.
- **Offset** — a consumer's bookmark, owned *by the consumer* (dumb broker, smart consumer). Rewind it and you replay history; that one property powers reprocessing, new-subscriber backfills, and [Kappa architecture](../data/analytics.md).
- **Retention** — the log keeps records for a configured time/size (or forever, with tiered storage spilling to [object storage](../data/object-storage.md)) *regardless of consumption*. Retention is a policy about history, not a side effect of reading. **Compacted topics** keep the latest record per key instead — a changelog that converges to a table (the natural home of CDC feeds and the outbox's downstream).

## Why it's absurdly fast

Sequential I/O all the way down: producers append batches, consumers read linearly, and the OS **page cache** does the real serving (the hot tail of each partition lives in RAM; brokers often push GB/s while their JVM heap does almost nothing — capacity-plan broker *memory as page cache*, not heap). Zero-copy `sendfile` moves bytes NIC-ward without touching userspace; producer-side batching + compression amortize everything. There's no magic — just an architecture that never asks the disk to seek.

## Durability: three configs that are really one contract

Each partition has a **leader** and replicas; the **ISR** (in-sync replica set) tracks who's caught up. Your durability is the interaction of three settings, and reciting the safe recipe verbatim is legitimate interview currency:

> **`replication.factor=3, acks=all, min.insync.replicas=2`** — a write acks only after the leader *and* at least one follower have it; you tolerate one broker's death with zero acknowledged-data loss, and if the ISR shrinks below 2, producers *block rather than lose data*.

The dials it's made of: `acks=0` (fire-and-forget), `acks=1` (leader only — leader dies pre-replication, acked data gone), `acks=all` (the ISR must have it). And the toggle that encodes [CAP](../foundations/cap-pacelc.md) in one boolean: **`unclean.leader.election`** — when every in-sync replica is dead, may a stale replica become leader? `true` = available but acked writes vanish; `false` = partition offline until an ISR member returns. Default is false; changing it is choosing A over C, and you should be able to say so.

Producer-side, **idempotent producers** (sequence numbers per producer/partition — broker dedupes retries) are table stakes, and **transactions** extend to atomic read-process-write *across Kafka topics* — the honest scope of Kafka's "exactly-once," dissected on the [next page](delivery-semantics.md).

## Consumer groups: both messaging patterns at once

A **consumer group** divides a topic's partitions among its members — within the group, competing consumers (work-queue semantics); each *additional group* independently reads the full stream (fan-out semantics). Both patterns, one log, no copies.

The operational physics: **max useful parallelism = partition count** (a 12-partition topic caps any group at 12 active consumers — the 13th idles), so partition count is a *capacity decision made at creation*. You can add partitions later but **adding partitions breaks the key→partition mapping** — records for user 42 start landing in a new partition while old ones sit in the old, so per-key *ordering across the boundary* is lost (plan generously up front; the standard sizing: target throughput ÷ ~10–20 MB/s per partition, then round up for growth and consumer parallelism, while remembering that hundreds of thousands of partitions cluster-wide have their own metadata/election costs).

**Rebalancing** — membership changes (deploy, crash, scale-out) redistribute partitions. The folklore fear ("rebalance storms") dates from stop-the-world rebalances; modern **cooperative incremental rebalancing** and **static membership** (a restarting pod reclaims its old partitions without triggering a full shuffle) mostly tamed it — but every deploy still causes a consumption pause per member, which is why consumer-lag graphs sawtooth on release days. **Lag** (and lag *age*) per group is the metric that matters — [same lesson as every queue](async-fundamentals.md).

## Where Kafka is the wrong answer

Saying this unprompted is a senior signal: **task queues with per-message semantics** (retry *this one* message with backoff, priority-jump that one, ack individually) — SQS/RabbitMQ do this natively; on Kafka you'd hand-build it against the grain of offsets. **Request/response** — it's a log, not an RPC bus. **Tiny scale** — a three-broker cluster plus schema governance for 200 messages/minute is an ops bill with no invoice line justifying it (a Postgres table with [`SKIP LOCKED`](../data/transactions.md) is the honest answer at that scale). Kafka earns its keep when you need *replayable history, multiple independent readers, per-key ordering at scale, or an integration backbone* — which is to say, when you need a log.

Ecosystem in one breath, since designs lean on it: **Connect** (source/sink connectors — [Debezium CDC](../data/analytics.md) lands here), **Streams/Flink** ([stateful processing](event-driven.md)), **Schema Registry** (Avro/Protobuf contracts with enforced compatibility — the [data-contract](../data/analytics.md) enforcement point), and **KRaft** (ZooKeeper's gone; Kafka self-manages metadata via its own Raft — one less system to operate).

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    The Kafka operator's dashboard: **consumer lag age per group** (the business metric), **ISR shrink/expand events** (a broker falling out of ISRs is sick — GC pauses, disk, network — and `min.insync.replicas` means shrinkage eventually *blocks producers*: availability incident with a durability cause), **under-replicated partitions** (the cluster-wide health scalar), **disk fill** (retention × ingest is your runway; the "someone set retention to forever" genre ends with a full disk, and a Kafka broker with a full disk is a very sad, very manual recovery), **rebalance frequency** (deploy hygiene), and **hot partitions** (skewed keys — [the same hot-key physics](../caching/failure-modes.md), detected per-partition, fixed by key salting or compound keys). Capacity trick worth repeating: brokers live on page cache — a consumer doing a deep historical replay evicts the hot tail and degrades *everyone*; that's why replays get throttled and why "add memory" is real Kafka capacity planning.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Kafka as the org's integration fabric is a governance project wearing infrastructure clothes** — without schema registry + compatibility enforcement + topic ownership + naming taxonomy, the shared log becomes distributed spaghetti with retention; the tech takes a quarter, the governance is forever. (2) **Retention is product strategy** — 7-day retention makes Kafka a buffer; infinite (tiered) retention makes it a system of record that downstream teams *rebuild state from* ([event sourcing's](event-driven.md) enabling condition); that choice reshapes DR, compliance, and cost, and deserves a decision record. (3) **Multi-tenancy needs quotas** — one team's backfill can starve the fleet's producers; per-client quotas and isolation tiers (the transactional cluster vs. the analytics cluster) are the [noisy-neighbor](../data/partitioning.md) answer. (4) **Run vs. buy** — managed Kafka (MSK/Confluent) removes broker toil but not the real work (partitioning strategy, schema governance, lag SLOs, topic taxonomy — all still yours); price the *remaining* work honestly when someone claims managed makes it free.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    **"Why Kafka over a queue?"** gets the reframe: *"It's a replicated log, not a queue — consumers own offsets, so I get replay, multiple independent readers, and per-key ordering at scale; retention decouples my downstream's availability from data loss."* Four properties, one sentence. Durability probe → recite the recipe (`RF=3, acks=all, min.isr=2`, tolerate one broker, producers block rather than lose) and name `unclean.leader.election` as the CAP toggle for bonus depth. Scaling probe → partition math out loud: *"300 MB/s ÷ ~15 MB/s per partition ≈ 20; provision 40–50 for growth and consumer parallelism, sized up front because adding partitions later breaks key ordering at the boundary."* Consumer semantics probe → groups explained as both patterns at once (divide within, fan-out across), lag-age as the alert. And keep the "when not Kafka" list loaded — task queues, request/response, tiny scale — because knowing a tool's off-switch is rarer signal than knowing its features.
