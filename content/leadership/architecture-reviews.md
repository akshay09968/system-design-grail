# Architecture Reviews

Run the arithmetic on a Staff+ calendar: you'll review ten designs for every one you author. That ratio makes review *the* mechanism by which your judgment scales — the place where one person's scar tissue becomes forty teams' prevented incidents. It's also a social craft with real failure modes: the review that rubber-stamps, the review that bikesheds, the reviewer who wins arguments and loses colleagues. This page is both halves — the technical toolkit and the human one.

## The reviewer's toolkit, consolidated

This site has been arming you for reviews all along; here is the kit assembled. Five questions find most problems in most designs:

1. **"What must be true for this to work?"** — surfaces the unstated assumptions: the [cache hit rate](../caching/failure-modes.md) the capacity plan silently requires, the [clock synchronization](../distributed/time-ordering.md) the dedup logic leans on, the vendor rate limit nobody checked.
2. **"What breaks first at 10×?"** — [the bottleneck sequence](../foundations/scalability.md), demanded rather than performed. Designs that can't answer haven't met their own load.
3. **"What's the blast radius when — not if — it fails?"** — [the four questions](../foundations/thinking-in-systems.md) per component, [correlated-failure hunting](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) ("what do the replicas share?"), and [the metastability probe](../distributed/failure-modes.md): "what sustains overload after the trigger clears?"
4. **"Why not the boring alternative?"** — the [innovation-token](technology-strategy.md) check. If the doc's [alternatives section](design-docs.md) can't make Postgres-and-a-queue lose fairly, the proposal is fashion.
5. **"Who operates this at 3 a.m.?"** — [observability, runbooks, on-call ownership](../observability/index.md), and the [org question](org-design.md) hiding inside: does the proposed boundary match a team that can actually carry the pager?

Behind those five, the deeper sweeps as time allows: [the coordination inventory](../distributed/coordination.md) ("does this really need agreement?"), [the cost paragraph's presence](../devops/cost-capacity.md), [the assume-breach walk](../security/defense-in-depth.md), [the migration path](technology-strategy.md) ("how do we get there from here — and back?"), and the one-way-door check (is the [shard key](../data/partitioning.md) / API shape / datastore choice getting ceremony proportional to its irreversibility?).

## Feedback that lands

The technical finding is worthless if the delivery makes the author defensive. The mechanics that keep reviews productive:

- **Critique the design, never the designer.** "This design loses writes during failover" opens an investigation; "you didn't think about failover" opens a fight.
- **Ask real questions before making statements.** "What happens to in-flight requests during the resharding?" is Socratic *and* genuinely humble — sometimes there's an answer you missed, and the question finds it either way. (Fake-Socratic gotcha questions, where you know and they know you know, read as theater. Ask because you want the answer.)
- **Rank your objections, out loud.** "Two blocking concerns, three preferences — the blockers are the failover story and the cost model; everything else, your call." Unranked feedback forces authors to treat nitpicks and showstoppers identically, which is how twenty-comment reviews produce zero changes. The blocking/non-blocking distinction is the single highest-value habit in review culture.
- **Bring the smaller version, not just the no.** A review that kills a design owes the author the salvage: "the full event-sourcing rebuild doesn't clear the bar, but [the CDC-plus-projections 80% version](../messaging/event-driven.md) gets your actual requirement for a tenth the cost." Killing projects kindly is a Staff skill; [the estimation kill](../foundations/estimation.md) ("that's 3 PB/year before we start") remains the gentlest weapon, because arithmetic isn't personal.
- **State confidence and what would change your mind.** "Strong opinions, loosely held" operationalized: "I'm 80% sure the fan-out breaks at celebrity scale — a load test on the follower distribution would settle it." That sentence invites evidence instead of escalation, and it models [calibration](../interviews/communication.md) for the room.
- **Disagree and commit — in that order, explicitly.** When you lose the argument: dissent on the record ([in the ADR](design-docs.md)), then *genuinely* commit — no relitigating in hallways, no told-you-so incident laps. The record protects you; the commitment protects the org.

And receiving review, since Staff+ authors are also the most-reviewed: steel-man the objection before answering it, thank the finder of the genuinely bad news (they just saved you a postmortem), and treat "I don't understand this section" as a writing bug, not a reader bug.

## Review as an institution

Individual reviews are judgment; the review *system* is governance, and someone at Staff+ has to own its health:

- **Match ceremony to blast radius** ([the two-way-door rule](design-docs.md), applied): lightweight async review for reversible choices; full-panel review for one-way doors. An org that reviews everything heavily reviews nothing well.
- **The production-readiness review (PRR)** is ops' structured contribution — a launch-gate checklist ([SLOs defined?](../observability/slos.md) [runbooks?](../observability/alerting.md) [capacity model?](../devops/cost-capacity.md) [rollback rehearsed?](../devops/deployments.md) [security walk done?](../security/defense-in-depth.md)) that converts this site's callouts into a gate. Checklists catch the forgettable; reviewers save their attention for the novel.
- **Watch the reviewer bottleneck.** When every design queues behind three trusted reviewers, review became the org's [serial fraction](../foundations/scalability.md). The fix is deliberate: delegate reviews *with* your rubric (this page is a training doc — use it), pair juniors into reviews as apprenticeship, and reserve the scarce senior attention for the one-way doors.
- **Mind the standards/judgment boundary.** Consistency questions ("we use Postgres unless waived") belong in [written standards](technology-strategy.md) enforced by paved roads, not relitigated per review; judgment questions (is *this* the right boundary?) deserve humans. Reviews that spend their minutes on the former starve the latter.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    Operational experience is the single scarcest input in most review rooms — deploy it deliberately: you are the person who asks about [connection-pool sizing under the failover](../networking/fundamentals.md), the [reconnect stampede after the deploy](../case-studies/chat.md), the [conntrack table on the NAT path](../devops/cloud-networking.md), and *who carries the pager* — questions whiteboard-native reviewers structurally miss. Two ops-specific review instruments: **demand the deploy story** (a design without its [rollout, rollback, and migration choreography](../devops/deployments.md) is a partial submission — send it back the way an editor returns a draft), and **read the on-call implications as a first-class cost** ("this adds a fourth stateful system to a team already at 2 pages/shift" is a blocking concern, [not a lifestyle complaint](org-design.md)). The PRR checklist above is yours to institutionalize — nobody else in the building will.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Review is your teaching medium** — the goal isn't catching this design's bugs but upgrading this *team's* next design; explain the principle behind the finding ([the four questions](../foundations/thinking-in-systems.md), not just today's instance) and the review compounds. (2) **Track your own calibration** — revisit your blocking objections quarterly: which materialized? A reviewer who blocks often and is rarely vindicated is spending credibility they'll need; the honest ledger keeps you sharp and keeps your "blocking" label meaningful. (3) **The quarterly read-across** — reviews are sensor data: the same gap appearing in five teams' designs (nobody writes cost sections; everyone hand-rolls retries) is a [platform gap or strategy gap](technology-strategy.md), not five coincidences. Reviews feed strategy the way [postmortems feed reliability](../observability/incidents.md). (4) **Protect dissent structurally** — the review culture where juniors safely question Staff designs is the one that catches Staff mistakes; ask "what's the strongest case against my own position?" out loud, and mean it.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    The design-review interview — critique a flawed architecture — is now standard in Staff+ loops, and it's this page performed under a clock. The winning structure: **inventory before attacking** (two minutes absorbing: restate the design's goal and shape — evaluators score comprehension first), **rank the top three risks** ("the blocking concerns are the [split-brain window](../data/replication.md) in this failover, the [hot-partition exposure](../data/partitioning.md) of this key choice, and the missing [degraded mode](../distributed/resilience.md); I'd also note, non-blocking, ..."), **probe with questions where the doc is silent** rather than assuming the worst, and **end with the salvage** — what you'd keep, the smaller version that works. The differentiator matching real reviews: *ranked* findings with confidence attached, versus the candidate who fires twenty equal-weight objections. And bring one review story to behavioral rounds: the design you blocked (and what happened), or better — the one you *un*-blocked by finding the smaller version.
