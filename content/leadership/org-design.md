# Engineering Org Design

Conway's law — systems mirror the communication structures of the organizations that build them — is usually quoted as a warning. At Staff+ it's a **tool**: since you ship your org chart regardless, design the org chart with the same rigor you'd design the system, because it *is* the system's first architecture diagram. This page treats teams the way the rest of the site treats services: boundaries, interfaces, capacity limits, and failure modes — because the [USL](../foundations/scalability.md) applies to humans with brutal fidelity, and coordination cost is the org's quadratic term.

## Conway's law, wielded

The passive version of Conway's law produces the classic pathology: an org of four teams builds a system with four components *whether or not four is the right number*, and every team boundary becomes a system boundary complete with [API friction](../networking/apis.md), [latency hops](../networking/fundamentals.md), and political ownership disputes. The active version — the **inverse Conway maneuver** — runs the causality deliberately: *choose the architecture you want, then shape teams to match it*, and let the org's natural mirroring work for you instead of against you.

The corollary that should be said in every architecture review: **every service boundary proposal is a team boundary proposal.** Microservices without matching team ownership is a distributed monolith with extra steps — you pay [the network tax](../networking/fundamentals.md) and the coordination tax *without* buying the independent-deployment benefit, because five teams still have to synchronize every release. The system decomposition and the org decomposition are one decision wearing two diagrams; drawing them separately is how both end up wrong.

## Team topologies: the four shapes

Skelton and Pais's *Team Topologies* gave the industry its working vocabulary — four team types, and the discipline is that there are only four:

- **Stream-aligned** — owns a product/user-journey slice end to end; the default type, the ones that ship value directly. Everything else exists to make these faster.
- **Platform** — builds [the paved roads](technology-strategy.md): the deploy pipeline, the observability stack, the golden-path templates. Product mindset mandatory ([adoption is the metric](technology-strategy.md)); its failure modes are the *ivory tower* (built without customers, adopted by nobody) and *ticket-ops* (a human queue with no leverage — [the platform is supposed to be self-serve](../devops/cicd.md)).
- **Enabling** — teaches and moves on: embeds with a team to install a capability ([SRE practices](../observability/index.md), security habits), then *leaves*. The leaving is definitional — an enabling team that stays is a dependency, not an enabler.
- **Complicated-subsystem** — owns the genuinely specialist piece (the ML ranking core, the [ledger](../case-studies/payments.md), the codec pipeline) where deep expertise beats broad ownership.

And three **interaction modes**: *collaboration* (working together — expensive, high-bandwidth, temporary by design), *X-as-a-service* (consuming through an interface — cheap, scalable), and *facilitating* (the enabling mode). The trap the vocabulary exists to catch: **everything drifting into collaboration** — every team pairing with every team on everything, which is coordination soup: the [all-to-all topology](../foundations/scalability.md) whose cost grows quadratically. Healthy orgs push interactions toward X-as-a-service over time — which is precisely what [good platform boundaries and team APIs](#team-apis-the-org-version-of-service-contracts) enable.

## Cognitive load: the real sizing metric

Teams aren't sized by headcount arithmetic; they're sized by **what fits in the team's collective head**. A team owning three services it understands deeply outperforms one owning nine it understands shallowly — shallow ownership is where [gray failures](../distributed/failure-modes.md) live undetected, where on-call becomes roulette, and where "we don't touch that one" systems accumulate. The overload symptoms are legible if you look: onboarding measured in quarters, [pager fatigue](../observability/alerting.md), rising [MTTR](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) on systems the team nominally owns, and the tell-tale "we'd need to ask the person who wrote it" (a [bus-factor](executive-craft.md) alarm ringing).

The design responses: shrink the surface (hand a service to a team with headroom — [an org migration, run with the migration playbook](technology-strategy.md)), or shrink the *load per service* — which is exactly what [platforms](technology-strategy.md) are for: every concern the paved road absorbs ([resilience defaults](../distributed/resilience.md), [pipeline templates](../devops/cicd.md), [identity](../devops/secrets-identity.md)) is cognitive load deleted from every stream-aligned team at once. Platform investment is cognitive-load arbitrage.

## Ownership and the on-call economics

**You build it, you run it** is the DevOps thesis applied to org design: the team that carries the pager for its own code closes the feedback loop that makes systems operable — [operability instincts](../foundations/devops-lens.md) grow precisely where the pain lands. The main variants: full ownership (build+run, the default to aim for), SRE-partnered (a reliability team engages with *the product team still owning the service* — Google's model, viable at scale, dangerous when it decays into "throw it over the wall"), and platform-mediated (the platform absorbs the infrastructure pager so product teams carry only their application's).

The arithmetic nobody escapes: **a humane on-call rotation needs ~6–8 people** (one week in six minimum; below that you're burning humans — [sustained pages degrade judgment](../observability/alerting.md) and then retention). That number is a *hard org-design constraint*: a three-person team cannot own a tier-0 service, no matter how skilled — the rotation math forbids it. Every proposal that creates a new stateful, paged system implicitly proposes the team to carry it; [reviews should read that cost out loud](architecture-reviews.md).

## Team APIs: the org version of service contracts

A team, like a service, needs a **published interface**: what it owns (services + [SLOs](../observability/slos.md)), how to request work (and the SLA on responses), its roadmap, its docs, its communication channels, its on-call boundary. Teams with clear APIs get consumed [X-as-a-service](#team-topologies-the-four-shapes); teams without them get consumed by meetings — every interaction escalates to synchronous collaboration because there's no contract to rely on. The same [additive-evolution discipline](../networking/apis.md) applies: changing a team's interface (ownership transfers, intake changes) deserves versioning, notice, and a migration path for its consumers.

And the change process itself: **a reorg is a migration of the human system** — the same playbook applies ([derisk with the hardest case, enable with clarity, finish completely](technology-strategy.md)), the same failure mode looms (the 90% reorg, where old and new structures both half-exist, is *worse than either* — two sources of truth for ownership means none). The cheapest reorg remains the one you avoid: **don't reorganize what an interface fix would solve** — if two teams fight at a boundary, try redrawing the API before redrawing the org chart.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    On-call is the org's [observability](../observability/fundamentals.md): **pager load per team is the single most honest org-health metric in existence** — it can't be gamed in a slide deck, it locates cognitive overload precisely, and its trend predicts attrition quarters ahead. Read the org through it: a team at 3+ pages/shift is under-platformed, over-scoped, or carrying someone else's [tech debt interest](technology-strategy.md); a *system* nobody wants to inherit in a reorg is telling you its real operability score. Ops also holds the org's coordination ledger: **incident retros reveal the true team boundaries** — who had to be paged into whose incident, where the runbook said "contact team X and wait" — [the postmortem read-across](../observability/incidents.md) is a Conway diagram of where the org chart and the architecture disagree. Bring that data to org-design conversations and you'll be the only person in the room arguing from evidence.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    Markers: (1) **Say the quiet part in reviews** — "this proposal creates a fourth service for a team of five; that's a topology decision, not just a technical one" — making the org cost of architecture explicit is a Staff move that managers will thank you for. (2) **Know the ratios as priors, not laws** — platform investment commonly lands at 10–20% of engineering (rising with org size), EM:IC spans of 5–8, and the 6–8 on-call floor; deviations aren't wrong, but they need reasons. (3) **Design team seams like shard keys** — [the same four properties](../data/partitioning.md): high cohesion inside, low coordination across, aligned with the change patterns ("what changes together lives together"), and *hard to re-cut later* — team boundaries are one-way-door-ish, so they get [one-way-door ceremony](design-docs.md). (4) **The inverse Conway is a Staff+ lever precisely because you don't control the org chart** — you control the *architecture proposals* that the org chart will eventually mirror; propose boundaries you'd want teams to have, and say so in the doc.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    Org questions enter loops at senior-Staff and above — *"how would you structure teams for this product?"* — and the winning move is treating it as system design, because it is: **requirements** (product streams, scale, the specialist cores) → **boundaries** (stream-aligned teams sized by cognitive load, a platform team once [the rule of three](technology-strategy.md) bites, complicated-subsystem teams only where expertise genuinely concentrates) → **interfaces** (team APIs, X-as-a-service defaults, where collaboration is temporarily intentional) → **failure modes** (the coordination hotspot you're watching, the on-call math, how the design evolves at 2× headcount). Name Conway explicitly and interviewers at this level relax — it signals you know the game. For CTO conversations, add the honest constraint: *"I'd shape this around the people we actually have, then evolve toward the target — org design with real humans is [migration](technology-strategy.md), not greenfield."* That sentence separates people who've done it from people who've read about it.
