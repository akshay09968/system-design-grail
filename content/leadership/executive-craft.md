# Executive Craft

The CTO's actual job is holding technology and business in productive tension — translating in both directions without losing fidelity in either. Everything below is translation craft: metrics the board can act on, budgets that survive the CFO, vendors negotiated from evidence, due diligence that finds the truth in two weeks, and the communication discipline that underwrites all of it — **never bring a surprise, never bring a mystery without a plan.** This is the layer where [the whole site](../foundations/devops-lens.md) becomes business language.

## Metrics that matter upward

Boards and executive peers can't act on p99s; they can act on **trends, risks, and unit economics**. The one-page engineering health report that works:

- **Delivery**: the DORA four — deploy frequency, lead time, change-failure rate, MTTR — as *trends*, because [they're architecture outputs](../devops/cicd.md), and their direction says whether the machine is improving.
- **Reliability**: [SLO attainment](../observability/slos.md) on the 3–5 journeys the business dies without, plus incident count/severity trend. Not uptime theater — budget language: "we spent 60% of checkout's error budget this quarter; here's what bought it."
- **Economics**: [unit cost](../devops/cost-capacity.md) trends (cost per order/user/GB) and gross-margin impact — the translation of every infrastructure decision into the language the room already speaks.
- **Risk posture**: time-to-patch criticals, [audit findings open/closed](../security/defense-in-depth.md), [DR test recency](../observability/chaos.md), key-person exposure. Risk reported *routinely* is governance; risk reported only in crises is a career event.
- **Anti-metrics, refused out loud**: lines of code, story points, utilization. When asked for them, offer the DORA set instead and explain why — teaching the board to read engineering is part of the job.

## Budgets: headcount, run-vs-grow, and the CFO conversation

Engineering budgets are ~70–80% people, so **the budget conversation is really the headcount-allocation conversation**: what fraction runs the business (keep-the-lights-on, [platform](technology-strategy.md), compliance) versus grows it (product bets)? Making that split *explicit* — "we're 35% run today; the platform investment takes it to 30% by year-end, releasing two teams' worth of capacity" — converts an opaque cost center into a managed portfolio. Cloud spend rides the same discipline: [unit economics, commitment curves, and the levers ranked](../devops/cost-capacity.md), presented as *forecast plus options* ("spend lands at $X on trend; levers A/B/C trade savings against velocity thus"). The relationship rule with finance: **surprises are the only unforgivable sin** — a CFO who trusts your forecasts funds your bets; one burned by month-end archaeology audits them.

## Vendor strategy: negotiate from telemetry

Enterprise agreements are won by whoever has better data about your usage — so be that party: [usage curves and growth forecasts](../devops/cost-capacity.md) turn list-price conversations into commitment-curve math. The contract traps to read for personally: **egress and exit costs** ([data gravity](../data/object-storage.md) is the real lock-in — price the leaving, not just the staying), auto-renew windows, seat minimums that outlive reorgs, and support tiers that gate the SLA you assumed. Portfolio-level discipline: know your [tier-0 vendors](../foundations/reliability-availability.md) (the ones whose outage is your outage — [DNS, CDN, cloud, identity](../networking/dns.md)) and hold a rehearsed posture per: multi-vendor where the [abstraction is cheap](../networking/cdn.md), documented-exit where it isn't, and *accepted-dependency-with-eyes-open* where the integration value wins — written down, [like every risk acceptance](../security/defense-in-depth.md).

## Technical due diligence: truth in two weeks

Whether acquiring, being acquired, or being invested in, tech DD is the same exam — and it's mostly *about people and honesty, not architecture aesthetics*:

- **The team**: bus factor on critical systems (who can safely change the [ledger](../case-studies/payments.md)?), key-person risk, hero culture (one name in every incident is fragility wearing a cape), attrition in the git history.
- **Architecture honesty**: ask for the diagram, then [trace one real request](../foundations/thinking-in-systems.md) with an engineer — the *gap* between diagram and reality is the debt measure. "What breaks at 10× users?" separates teams that know their system from teams that hope.
- **Operational maturity**: [postmortems exist and are blameless?](../observability/incidents.md) [Deploys automated and frequent?](../devops/deployments.md) [Restore actually tested?](../data/sql-at-scale.md) Who has prod access? These four questions predict integration cost better than any code review.
- **The registers**: [tech debt](technology-strategy.md), [risk](#risk-and-governance), dependency/licensing (the GPL surprise genre), [security posture](../security/defense-in-depth.md). An org with honest registers is buyable; an org with none is an archaeology project priced as a company.
- Red flags that reprice deals: everything-depends-on-the-founder's-laptop, no [staging environment](../devops/cicd.md), compliance claims without evidence, and — subtle but damning — *no one can say why past decisions were made* ([the ADR argument](design-docs.md), now with a valuation attached).

## Risk and governance

The executive risk instrument is the **register**: each risk with likelihood × impact, a named owner, the mitigation state, and a review cadence — [the SLO discipline](../observability/slos.md) applied to uncertainty. The CTO-specific craft is *framing*: *compliance as market access* (SOC 2 is a sales unlock, not a tax — [the platform you built](../devops/secrets-identity.md) makes the audit a report), *security investment sized to the threat model* ([controls scale to attacker budget](../security/defense-in-depth.md), argued with the same actuarial honesty as [chaos spend](../observability/chaos.md)), and *accepted risks stated in writing* — "we accept single-region operation until Series C; here's the exposure math" is governance; the same fact undocumented is negligence with better vibes.

## Crisis leadership: the seat above the incident

In a SEV1, the executive's job is everything *except* the keyboard: **decision rights** (pre-delegated: the IC runs the response, [full stop](../observability/incidents.md) — an exec who grabs the keyboard beheads the command structure), **air cover** (absorbing the board/customer pressure so responders work), **the external cadence** ([communicate on rhythm, not on progress](../observability/incidents.md) — customers, execs, and if needed press, each with calibrated honesty), and **the aftermath posture**: blameless upward too — the CTO who ships the [systemic-fix narrative](../observability/incidents.md) ("here's the class of failure and the investment that removes it") converts the worst week into the reliability budget's best argument. Crises are also when the org learns what you're made of; the calm is the job.

## Communicating up: the memo discipline

The mechanics that keep executive trust compounding: **BLUF always** ([bottom line up front](design-docs.md) — an exec summary a board member can act on without reading further); **three options, one recommendation** (never a mystery without a plan, never a single option disguised as a choice); **pre-reads over presentations** ([the design-doc culture](design-docs.md), promoted to the boardroom); and **trend + risk + ask** as the standing update format — what's moving, what could hurt us, what I need from you. The compounding asset is *calibration*: the executive whose "this worries me" has always been worth worrying about gets believed at the moment it matters most.

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    An operations background is quietly a CTO asset — the translations are already native: **cost fluency** ([the bill as architecture](../devops/cost-capacity.md)) is the CFO bridge; **risk realism** (you've *seen* the failure modes, so your risk register is grounded, not theoretical); **crisis calm** ([incident command](../observability/incidents.md) is exec-crisis training with receipts); and **evidence habits** ([SLOs, error budgets, postmortems](../observability/index.md)) are governance instincts most executives have to be taught. The gap to close deliberately: revenue-side fluency — how the product makes money, what customers churn over, what sales promises — because a CTO who only speaks cost-and-risk becomes the department of no. Spend real time with sales calls and churn analyses; [the strategy kernel](technology-strategy.md) needs a business diagnosis, not just an infrastructure one.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    This page is also the Staff/Principal job description one level early — the **right-hand archetype** ([the path](the-path.md)) does exactly this work without the title: drafting the board slide's technical section, running the DD checklist on the acquisition target, writing [the memo](design-docs.md) that becomes the strategy. Three ways to practice from the current seat: (1) **write the quarterly engineering-health one-pager for your area** (DORA + SLO + unit cost + risks) whether anyone asked or not — it will get forwarded upward, with your name on it; (2) **sit in on a vendor negotiation or audit** — the mechanics are learnable only by exposure; (3) **translate one incident into business language** for a non-technical audience and watch which framings land. The Staff engineer who can already speak this dialect is the one in the room when the CTO conversation happens.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    CTO/VP conversations are narrative screens with business-fluency checks woven in: *"walk me through a technology strategy you set"* ([the kernel, with outcomes](technology-strategy.md)); *"tell me about your hardest incident"* — told from the command seat: decision rights, comms cadence, the systemic fix, the customer outcome; *"how would you assess our platform in your first 90 days?"* — the DD checklist above, framed as listen-first ("team, honesty gap, operational maturity, registers — and I'd ship one visible fix in the first month"); and the economics screen, often disguised as small talk: *"what's your take on our infra spend?"* wants [unit economics instinct](../devops/cost-capacity.md), not a number. Preparation is the same [three written stories](the-path.md) plus one addition — **a point of view on the business**: a CTO candidate without an opinion on how the company should win is interviewing for the wrong room. And at every level below CTO, this material compounds too: the Staff candidate who can say "here's how I'd explain this migration to the board" has just answered a question nobody else in the loop can.
