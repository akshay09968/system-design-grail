# Time & Ordering

"What time is it?" is a trick question in a distributed system. Two machines' clocks *never* agree — the only variables are by how much and whether your design notices. Everything downstream of that fact is this page: the bugs that wall clocks cause, the logical clocks invented to escape them, the atomic-clock exotica Google built to buy time back, and the practical patterns (single writers, sequence numbers, fencing) that let ordinary systems mostly sidestep the whole problem.

## Physical clocks: precise, confident, and wrong

Quartz oscillators drift — tens of parts per million, which compounds to *seconds per day* unsynchronized. **NTP** herds them back: millisecond-ish accuracy over the internet, sub-millisecond on a good LAN (and cloud providers now offer PTP/precision clocks doing microseconds). But the herding has sharp edges every operator eventually cuts themselves on:

- **Clocks step backward.** An NTP correction can yank a fast clock back; a VM migration or hypervisor pause can jump time either way. Any code computing `end - start` with wall clocks will eventually log a **negative duration** — the classic bug. The rule: **wall clocks for timestamps, monotonic clocks for intervals**, no exceptions (every language has both; most codebases mix them freely until the first impossible metric).
- **Processes pause.** GC stops the world; VMs freeze for live migration; laptops sleep. Between two adjacent lines of your code, *minutes* can pass. Hold that thought for the lease story below — it's the best story in this section.
- **Leap seconds** once crashed real systems at midnight; the industry's fix — **smearing** (spreading the extra second across hours) — means different providers' clocks deliberately diverge during the smear. Trivia with an operational moral: even the authorities disagree about what time it is.

**Where skewed clocks silently corrupt:** [last-write-wins conflict resolution](../data/replication.md) — node A's clock runs 200 ms fast; B's genuinely-later write loses to A's earlier one; data vanishes with no error anywhere (the Cassandra LWW horror genre — and why "whose clock?" is the first question to ask any LWW scheme). Also: token/cert expiry checks (clock skew = mysterious auth failures — the "cert not yet valid" incident), rate-limit windows, cron overlap, and cross-host log forensics ("logs lie across hosts — trust trace IDs, not timestamps" belongs in every runbook).

## Logical clocks: ordering without time

The 1978 escape hatch (Lamport's, and still the most cited idea in the field): if you can't agree on *when*, agree on *what came before what*. **Happens-before** is defined by causality — A happens-before B if they're in one process in order, or A sent a message B received, or transitivity. Everything else is **concurrent** — genuinely neither-first, which is the concept wall clocks can't express.

**Lamport clocks** — each process keeps a counter: increment on every event; stamp messages; on receive, set counter to `max(local, received) + 1`. Five lines, and you get: *if A happened-before B, then L(A) < L(B)*. What you don't get is the converse — L(A) < L(B) doesn't prove causality (they might be concurrent). Lamport timestamps give a *consistent total order* (great for "everyone processes ops in the same sequence") but can't *detect* concurrency.

**Vector clocks** — a counter *per node*, merged element-wise. Now comparison tells the truth: strictly-greater = causally after; incomparable = **concurrent** — which is exactly the "two writes conflict, someone must merge" detector that [Dynamo-lineage stores](../data/nosql.md) need. The cost: O(N) metadata per record that grows with participants — why real systems prune, bound, or (Cassandra's choice) skip them for per-cell LWW and accept this page's horror genre as a documented trade.

**Hybrid logical clocks (HLC)** — the modern synthesis: timestamps that *look like* wall time (useful for humans, TTLs, and "as-of" queries) but never violate happens-before (logical component breaks ties and rides through skew). CockroachDB and MongoDB run on them; if you're designing a distributed store in 2026, HLC is the sensible default answer to "what's in your timestamp?"

## TrueTime: buying certainty with atomic clocks

Google's Spanner asked a different question: what if we made the *uncertainty itself* explicit? **TrueTime** returns not a timestamp but an interval — [earliest, latest] — kept tight (single-digit ms) by GPS receivers and atomic clocks in every datacenter. Then the elegant move: to guarantee transaction A's timestamp precedes B's in *real* time, Spanner **commit-waits** — after choosing a commit timestamp, it *waits out the uncertainty window* before acknowledging, so no later transaction anywhere can get an earlier stamp. Result: **external consistency** (strict serializability — [the crown from the isolation page](../data/transactions.md)) across a planet.

Two portable lessons, even if you'll never own an atomic clock: **money buys tighter bounds, never zero** — every clock claim is an interval, and honest systems carry the interval instead of pretending it's a point; and **you can trade latency for ordering** — commit-wait is the purest example of "pay a known small cost to make a whole class of anomalies impossible," a trade template worth reusing ([AWS's TimeSync and precision-clock offerings](../devops/index.md) are dragging this capability toward everyone).

## Ordering in practice: mostly, don't

The patterns that let ordinary systems sidestep clock philosophy:

- **Single writer = free total order.** One process assigning sequence numbers ends the debate — this is [the log](../messaging/kafka.md): a Kafka partition is an *ordering service*; the [replication leader](../data/replication.md) is an ordering service; "route it through one place" is the oldest and best ordering algorithm. Its scaling limit is [Amdahl's serial fraction](../foundations/scalability.md), which is why the real pattern is *per-key single writers* (partitions).
- **Snowflake IDs** — the practical ID generator: 41 bits of milliseconds + 10 bits of node ID + 12 bits of per-node sequence = 64-bit IDs, roughly time-sortable, coordination-free, 4M/sec/node. The detail that shows real understanding: **what happens when the clock steps backward?** (The generator must refuse/wait — emitting IDs "from the past" breaks uniqueness; every serious implementation has this branch, and mentioning it is the nugget.)
- **Don't order — converge.** [CRDTs](../data/replication.md) make order irrelevant by construction (commutative merges); [idempotent, absolute-state events](../messaging/delivery-semantics.md) make replays and reorderings harmless. The cheapest ordering problem is the one you designed out.

## The story to remember: the pause and the lease

The canonical parable stitching this page to the [next](coordination.md): a client acquires a distributed lock (a lease expiring in 10 s), then hits a 15-second GC pause. It wakes *believing it holds the lock* — its next line of code executes under that assumption — while the lease has expired and another client legitimately holds it. Two writers, one resource, no clock was even wrong. The moral: **a process can never trust its own sense of elapsed time**, so correctness can't ride on "I checked the time before the pause." The fix — fencing tokens — headlines the coordination page; the lesson lands here: *any* lease/timeout/TTL logic must ask "what if the process pauses between check and act?"

!!! ops "DevOps lens"
    Clock hygiene is unglamorous and load-bearing: **monitor NTP offset and sync state on every host** (alert at tens of ms; an unsynced fleet member is a data-corruption lottery ticket for LWW stores and a forensic nightmare for logs), pin VMs/containers to proper time sources (and know your cloud's PTP options for the paths that care), and watch the **cert/token clock-skew incident genre** ("works from my machine, 401s from that AZ" — check the clock before the auth config). During incident forensics, distrust cross-host timestamp ordering on principle: sub-second sequences reconstructed from logs on different machines are fiction; [trace IDs and explicit causality](../observability/tracing.md) are the record. And add the *pause* class to your mental model of any timeout-bearing system you operate: GC logs and hypervisor steal-time belong on the same dashboard as the lease expiries they explain.

!!! staff "Staff+ altitude"
    The Staff move is the **wall-clock dependence audit**: inventory where the architecture *assumes* synchronized time — LWW resolution, TTL semantics, token expiry windows, scheduler triggers, "process events in timestamp order" logic — and for each, either bound the skew (monitored NTP/PTP with alerting as a *stated* system dependency), redesign onto logical/sequence ordering, or document the corruption window as an accepted risk. Three altitude markers: (1) treat commit-wait as a *template* — "pay bounded latency to delete an anomaly class" generalizes far beyond Spanner; (2) for platforms where time is truth (finance, tracing, audit), clock infrastructure (PTP, GPS-disciplined sources) is a platform investment with an owner, not a default; (3) in design reviews of anything lease-based, ask the pause question verbatim — *"what happens if the process sleeps 30 seconds right here?"* — it's the cheapest formal-methods substitute available and it finds real bugs weekly.

!!! interview "In the interview"
    Set pieces: **Lamport in 60 seconds** (counter, stamp, max+1 on receive; gives consistent order, can't detect concurrency — vector clocks add the per-node vector precisely to detect it); **the LWW reflex** — any time someone (including you) says "latest write wins," append "…by whose clock? Skew silently drops newer writes; I'd want per-key single writers or HLC timestamps" ; **Snowflake structure** on demand (41+10+12, time-sortable, and the clock-backward branch as your depth nugget); and **the GC-pause lease story** told well — it's thirty seconds, it's true, and it sets up [fencing tokens](coordination.md) so naturally that interviewers often just ask "so how do you fix it?", which is exactly where you want the conversation going. Meta-signal: candidates who reach for *ordering-by-design* (single writers, sequences, convergence) over *ordering-by-clock* read as people who've debugged the difference.
