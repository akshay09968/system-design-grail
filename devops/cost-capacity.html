    Cost & Capacity¶

    At scale, the cloud bill is your architecture diagram with dollar signs on the arrows — every fan-out, every cross-AZ hop, every retention policy is a line item. Cost is the sixth design axis, the one Staff+ interviews increasingly probe directly, and the one DevOps engineers are uniquely positioned to own — because you've seen the bill, and most engineers genuinely have not.

    Unit economics: the number that makes cost engineering¶

    Absolute bills mislead ("spend doubled!" — and traffic tripled: that's improvement). The metric that thinks clearly is cost per unit of value: per request, per active user, per GB ingested, per build minute. Unit costs turn cost work into engineering: they trend, they compare against price (gross margin per user is an architecture output), and they localize problems ("cost per request rose 40% after the March release" is a diff-able statement — deploy markers belong on cost dashboards too). This framing is estimation-page math pointed at money, and opening a cost conversation with units instead of totals is an instant seniority marker.

    The levers, ranked by ROI¶

    1. Delete things. Unattached volumes, forgotten environments, zombie load balancers, logs retained forever by default, the staging cluster nobody's touched since 2024. Every org's first cost sweep finds 15–30% of pure waste — the "turn it off" dividend, no architecture required. (Storage is the silent accumulator: it never complains, so it never shrinks — retention policy is cost policy.)
    2. Rightsizing. The stranded-capacity report: requested-vs-used per workload, VPA recommendations applied, instances resized to measured reality. This is where request honesty becomes money.
    3. Pricing models, layered. Commitments (reserved/savings plans) for the measured baseline — 30–60% off for a promise you were going to keep anyway; spot/preemptible for interruption-tolerant work — 60–90% off, earned through discipline: diversified instance pools, graceful drain on the two-minute warning, checkpointing for long jobs, stateless-or-checkpointed only. The mature fleet is a layer cake: committed baseline, on-demand for the daily wave, spot for batch tiers and CI runners.
    4. Storage tiering & lifecycle — the object-storage ladder, automated, owned, verified.
    5. Topology & egress — NAT paths, endpoints, cross-AZ chatter, CDN hit rate as a cost lever (origin egress ∝ miss rate), zone-aware routing. The network diagram is an invoice.
    6. Architecture-level moves — caching as cost avoidance (every hit is a compute+DB dollar unspent), async smoothing (queues convert peak-provisioned capacity into average-provisioned + a backlog — you buy capacity for the mean, not the max), approximation (HLL, sampling — exactness has a unit cost; spend it only where the business needs it), and the serverless crossover: per-request pricing wins for spiky/low-volume (scale-to-zero: you pay for use) and loses for sustained load (the per-invocation premium × always-on traffic > provisioned) — do the arithmetic at your traffic shape; both "all-in on Lambda" and "never serverless" are positions that skipped the math.

    Capacity planning: the loop with lead times¶

    Autoscaling handles minutes; capacity planning handles quarters. The loop: measure current utilization → model what drives it (traffic × per-unit cost — Little's law and the estimation recipes, again) → forecast (growth curve + launches + seasonality) → provision ahead of lead time → verify with load tests (honest ones).

    The two operator truths interviews reward: quotas are outages waiting — cloud account limits (instances, IPs, API rates) are silent ceilings that autoscaling hits at peak; audit and raise them ahead of forecasts (the quota-denied scale-out during your biggest day is a fully preventable postmortem — and specialized capacity like GPUs has procurement lead times measured in months, which makes forecasting a hard dependency, not an exercise). And headroom is a policy, not a leftover — utilization targets per tier (latency-sensitive: 40–60% for the hockey stick's sake; batch: 80%+; surviving-region math for multi-region) written down, so the cost-vs-resilience tension is decided rather than discovered.

    FinOps as a practice¶

    The organizational layer that makes the levers pull-able: allocation first (tagging/labeling enforced as policy-code — untagged spend is unowned spend, and unowned spend only grows), showback then chargeback (teams see their bill before they're billed it; visibility alone changes behavior), anomaly detection (the recursive-logging loop, the runaway BigQuery scan, the replication doubling — caught in days, not at month-end archaeology), and cost in design review — a costed paragraph next to the architecture diagram ("~$0.0004/request at projected volume: 60% compute, 25% egress, 15% storage — the fan-out is the driver"), normalized until it's as expected as the failure-modes section.

    Numbers worth carrying (order-of-magnitude, for live arithmetic): general-purpose compute ~$0.03–0.05/vCPU-hour on-demand; internet egress ~$0.05–0.09/GB (the CDN's is cheaper); NAT processing ~$0.045/GB; cross-AZ ~$0.01–0.02/GB; S3 standard ~$0.023/GB-month, deep archive ~$0.001. With these five, you can price a whiteboard design to one significant figure in real time — which is exactly the party trick estimation promised.

    DevOps lens

    The operational cadence: cost dashboards per team/service with deploy markers (cost regressions are regressions — bisect them like latency), budget alerts tiered (forecast-based warnings before threshold-based alarms), spot interruption drills (if reclaim waves haven't been rehearsed, your "60% savings" carries an availability IOU — PDBs, drain handling, diversification verified under fire), and month-end archaeology eliminated by month-long attribution (the bill should contain zero surprises by the time it arrives). The incident genres: the quota wall at peak (audit quarterly, raise ahead of launches), the anomaly that ran 26 days (alert on velocity, not just totals), and the savings that broke prod (the rightsizing sweep that removed the headroom the hockey stick needed — cost changes are production changes; canary them).

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) Cost budgets like error budgets — a unit-cost target per system with alerting on trend, so efficiency is a maintained property, not a annual crusade; the analogy to SLOs is exact: budget, burn rate, policy response. (2) The judgment line — premature optimization wastes engineers on pennies; negligent waste burns margin at scale: the Staff skill is knowing which regime each system is in (a $200/month service deserves zero optimization; a $2M/year pipeline deserves a dedicated effort), and redirecting energy accordingly. (3) Commit negotiation with usage curves — enterprise discounts are priced against your forecast; the engineer who owns the capacity model owns the negotiation leverage. (4) The repatriation question, held honestly — at extreme sustained scale the cloud-vs-metal math can flip (the famous exits are real, and so are the failed ones); the Staff posture is neither dogma: model your workload's shape (sustained vs. spiky, egress-heavy vs. compute-heavy), include the ops headcount and undifferentiated heavy lifting honestly, and revisit at scale milestones.

    In the interview

    Volunteer cost like you volunteer failure modes — it's the same reflex, aimed at the other invisible axis: "worth noting this fan-out triples internal traffic — fine in-AZ, real money cross-AZ, so I'd keep replication zone-aware"; "retention drives this design: 30 days hot is ~80 TB, a year in archive is a rounding error, so the tiering policy is the actual decision." The set pieces: serverless crossover math on demand ("at 50 req/s sustained, per-invocation pricing loses to two small always-on nodes — at 50 req/day, it's free; the traffic shape decides"); spot-with-discipline for any batch/CI tier ("60–90% off, paid for with drain handling and pool diversification"); and the unit-economics opener for any "reduce costs" prompt ("first: cost per request and its trend — then the ranked levers: delete, rightsize, commit, tier, topology, architecture"). At Staff level, one costed sentence per design section is the signal — it says you've owned a system long enough to have met its invoice.
