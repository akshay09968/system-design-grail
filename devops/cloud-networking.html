    Cloud Networking¶

    Every arrow in every design you've drawn assumes a network path — and cloud networking is where those paths get made real, priced by the gigabyte, and broken by a security group rule nobody documented. This is home turf for operators and a blind spot for almost everyone else in the interview pool: candidates who can place their design into an actual VPC shape, name the NAT bill, and explain why peering doesn't scale are describing systems they've run, not drawn.

    VPC anatomy, and the mistake that lasts a decade¶

    A VPC is your private address space (a CIDR block) carved into subnets (each in one AZ), stitched by route tables, reaching the internet through an internet gateway (public subnets) or NAT gateway (private subnets, outbound-only).

    The mistake to name before anyone asks: overlapping CIDR ranges. Every team that ever picked 10.0.0.0/16 by default discovers, years later, that they cannot peer, cannot VPN to the acquired company, cannot mesh — because addresses collide and NAT-ing around it is misery. Address-space planning (IPAM: non-overlapping ranges allocated per team/region/environment from day one) is one of the rare decisions with a decade of consequence, which is why it appears again in the Staff callout. Modern pressure makes it worse: Kubernetes gives every pod an IP, and a serious cluster eats a /16 for breakfast — plan pod CIDRs like you mean it.

    The standard three-tier shape, worth drawing reflexively: public subnets hold only load balancers and NAT/egress infrastructure; private subnets hold all compute; isolated subnets hold data stores (no route to the internet at all). Blast-radius-by-routing — the failure-domain instinct expressed as route tables.

    Security groups: the firewall that references itself¶

    Security groups are stateful, instance-attached, allow-only firewalls — and their killer idiom is SG-to-SG references: "the database SG allows 5432 from the app-tier SG" — membership-based rules that survive autoscaling, IP churn, and redeploys, because they name roles, not addresses. (NACLs — stateless, subnet-level — exist; the honest guidance is that most organizations get everything they need from SGs plus isolated subnets, and NACL rule-pair bookkeeping is where afternoons go to die.) The posture throughout: default-deny, open specific flows, and manage SG rules as IaC — the undocumented hand-added rule is both a security finding and, six months later, the drift that breaks something when "cleaned up."

    The NAT bill, and the endpoints that delete it¶

    The DevOps-special topic interviews never cover and production always does: NAT gateways charge per GB processed, and private subnets reach everything external through them — including, by default, S3, container registries, and other cloud services. The genre incident: a busy cluster pulling images and shipping data to S3 through NAT racks up a five-figure monthly charge for traffic that never left the provider's network.

    The fixes, in order: gateway VPC endpoints for S3/DynamoDB (free, route-table magic — there is no reason not to have these on day one), interface endpoints / PrivateLink for other services (per-hour + per-GB, still usually a bargain vs. NAT), per-AZ NAT gateways (cross-AZ traffic to a NAT in another zone pays twice — cross-AZ charges plus NAT processing), and consolidated, inspected egress for what genuinely must leave.

    PrivateLink deserves its own sentence because it changed service topology: it exposes one service (behind an NLB) into consumers' VPCs as a local interface — no peering, no route sharing, no network adjacency, non-transitive by construction. It's how SaaS vendors reach enterprise VPCs and how internal platform teams expose services across organizational trust boundaries: service-level connectivity instead of network-level — the least-privilege instinct applied to topology.

    Connecting networks: peering's ceiling and the hub¶

    VPC peering joins two VPCs — and is non-transitive: A↔B and B↔C gives A no path to C. At three VPCs this is a footnote; at thirty it's n(n−1)/2 peerings and a route-table management crisis — the coordination-cost curve, drawn in network links. The scaling answer is Transit Gateway (hub-and-spoke: every VPC attaches once, routing is central, transitive by design, per-GB priced), plus VPN (quick, internet-riding, IPsec) or Direct Connect/Interconnect (dedicated private circuits: consistent latency and lower per-GB for serious hybrid volume) toward on-prem. Private DNS ties it together — split-horizon zones resolving internal names to internal paths, endpoint DNS steering service calls into PrivateLink instead of the public internet.

    DevOps lens

    The tools that make cloud networks debuggable: flow logs (per-ENI connection records — enable on critical VPCs before the incident, because the forensic question is always "what talked to what at 03:14," and you can't retro-enable history), reachability analyzers (the cloud's static analysis for "why can't A reach B" — beats tcpdump archaeology for SG/route/NACL misconfigurations), and the discipline of SG hygiene (named, tagged, IaC-managed, with a linter refusing 0.0.0.0/0 ingress — policy-as-code again). The incident genres: the silent SG block (new service, works locally, times out in staging — an SG reference nobody added; check reachability first, not the app), the asymmetric path (works one direction — route table or NACL statelessness), the conntrack-full NAT (new connections vanish, established ones fine), and the DNS-throttled resolver (VPC resolvers have per-ENI query limits; ndots amplification finds them).

    Staff+ altitude

    Markers: (1) Address space is a decade asset — own the IPAM: non-overlapping allocations, room for acquisitions, pod-CIDR budgets; the org that plans addresses in year one skips the re-IP migration in year six (a project with negative fun and zero feature value). (2) Topology is org design — shared-VPC (central network team owns the fabric, teams get subnets) vs. VPC-per-team (autonomy, connected via TGW + PrivateLink): the choice is Conway's law with route tables, and the modern default is per-domain VPCs with service-level (PrivateLink) rather than network-level (peering) integration — smaller trust surfaces, cleaner boundaries. (3) Egress architecture is a security posture — all outbound through inspected, allowlisted egress points, decided fleet-wide; "every subnet NATs wherever" is both a bill and an exfiltration surface. (4) Network cost is a design-review line item — cross-AZ chatter, NAT paths, and inter-region replication belong priced in the doc, because the topology diagram is the invoice.

    In the interview

    The 30-second placement that upgrades any design: "Three tiers per AZ across three AZs — LBs in public subnets, services in private, databases in isolated with no internet route; SG-to-SG rules so the DB only accepts from the app tier; S3 and DynamoDB via gateway endpoints so that traffic never touches NAT; everything else egresses through per-AZ NAT plus an allowlisting proxy." That paragraph quietly demonstrates security posture, cost awareness, and failure-domain thinking at once. Depth nuggets on demand: the NAT-bill story (image pulls through NAT — the fix is endpoints; instant ops credibility), peering non-transitivity ("A-B and B-C doesn't give A-C — at thirty VPCs that's a TGW hub, not六 hundred peerings"), and PrivateLink as service-level connectivity for exposing one service across trust boundaries. Probes to expect: "how do the services reach S3 privately?" (gateway endpoint, free, route-table entry); "how would a partner reach this API without VPN?" (PrivateLink producer/consumer); "why is your data tier unreachable from the internet — walk the layers" (no IGW route + SG default-deny + isolated subnet: defense in depth, named layer by layer).
